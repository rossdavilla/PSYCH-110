Chapter 7

Conception, Pregnancy, and Childbirth

The real biology of how a pregnancy begins, unfolds across three trimesters, and ends in birth and recovery — and why so much of what movies taught us about it is wrong.

📝 Study Guide 🔑 Key Terms

The Story We're Told

You already know how this story goes. Millions of sperm line up like sprinters, a gun goes off, and the fastest, strongest swimmer muscles to the front and wins the egg. Nine months later, the water breaks while mom is shopping for groceries. She's whisked off to the hospital for a "natural" birth — one without medication or machines — which is, of course, the best way to bring a baby into the world. A baby is born, gets laid on its mother's chest in the first golden minutes, and a bond snaps into place that will shape them both for the rest of their lives.

Here is the problem. Every one of those things is either flat wrong or so oversimplified that it's downright misleading. This is the way pregnancy and childbirth are portrayed in movies and TV shows, but real life is much more varied, messy, and complicated.

That gap is what this chapter is about. Conception, pregnancy, and childbirth are some of the most crucial but misunderstood events in human life — wrapped in folklore, marketing, and well-meaning advice that often has the facts backward. As you'll soon read, the true version is far more interesting than the montage.

7.1 Conception

Conception is the starting line of every pregnancy, and it is also where the myths begin. The popular picture — a frantic race won by the single strongest swimmer — gets almost every detail wrong. What actually happens is stranger, slower, and more selective than the cartoon suggests.

The Journey to the Egg: It's Not a Race

You've probably seen the cartoon version of conception. There's a race between millions of sperm, with only the fastest one claiming victory! It's a great story. It's also mostly wrong (Eisenbach & Giojalas, 2006)Full ReferenceEisenbach, M., & Giojalas, L. C. (2006). Sperm guidance in mammals: An unpaved road to the egg. Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology, 7(4), 276–285..

Start with the numbers. A typical ejaculate holds 100 to 300 million sperm. Of those, only a few hundred ever make it anywhere near the egg (Williams et al., 1993)Full ReferenceWilliams, M., Hill, C. J., Scudamore, I., Dunphy, B., Cooke, I. D., & Barratt, C. L. R. (1993). Sperm numbers and distribution within the human Fallopian tube around ovulation. Human Reproduction, 8(12), 2019–2026.. The trip is brutal, and most sperm never get past the early obstacles. But the survivors don't simply out-sprint everyone else. They're guided, and they're filtered.

Think of the female reproductive tract less like a racetrack and more like a long trail with a series of checkpoints and a helpful but picky trail guide. The tract screens out sperm that are damaged or poorly shaped. Only sperm of high quality are allowed to reach the egg.

Sperm aren't even ready to fertilize anything when they first arrive. They have to be "switched on" inside the female tract through a ripening step called capacitationiCapacitationThe final maturation step sperm undergo inside the female reproductive tract that makes them capable of fertilizing an egg.View in glossary →. Picture hikers reaching the trailhead and only then unpacking and gearing up for the real climb (Eisenbach & Giojalas, 2006)Full ReferenceEisenbach, M., & Giojalas, L. C. (2006). Sperm guidance in mammals: An unpaved road to the egg. Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology, 7(4), 276–285..

Once they're geared up, how do the sperm find their way? Researchers have found three guidance systems working together:

  • Swimming against the current (rheotaxisiRheotaxisThe tendency of sperm to orient and swim against the direction of fluid flow in the reproductive tract, which happens to steer them toward the egg.View in glossary →). Fluid in the tract flows gently in one direction, and sperm instinctively turn and swim against it, like a fish nosing upstream. Heading into the current happens to point them the right way (Miki & Clapham, 2013)Full ReferenceMiki, K., & Clapham, D. E. (2013). Rheotaxis guides mammalian sperm. Current Biology, 23(6), 443–452..
  • Following the warmth (thermotaxisiThermotaxisMovement guided by temperature; sperm sense the slightly warmer region near the egg and steer toward the heat.View in glossary →). The spot where the egg waits is slightly warmer than where the sperm pause to gear up. Sperm can sense that tiny temperature difference and steer toward the heat (Bahat et al., 2003)Full ReferenceBahat, A., Tur-Kaspa, I., Gakamsky, A., Giojalas, L. C., Breitbart, H., & Eisenbach, M. (2003). Thermotaxis of mammalian sperm cells: A potential navigation mechanism in the female genital tract. Nature Medicine, 9(2), 149–150..
  • Following the scent (chemotaxisiChemotaxisMovement directed by chemical signals; sperm detect molecules released near the egg and home in on their source.View in glossary →). As they get close, sperm pick up chemical signals given off near the egg, almost like following a smell to its source, and home in (Eisenbach & Giojalas, 2006)Full ReferenceEisenbach, M., & Giojalas, L. C. (2006). Sperm guidance in mammals: An unpaved road to the egg. Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology, 7(4), 276–285..

So, the real story isn't a brawl won by the toughest sperm or fastest swimmer. It's a guided journey, with the egg and the tract sending out signals that steer a small, healthy group of travelers toward the finish line. Among the sperm that make it, is the "winner" basically random, or does the female body get a say in which one succeeds? Scientists are still working that out.

A handful of recent studies hint that it may not be random. In lab experiments, researchers mixed sperm from different men with fluid from different women's reproductive tracts. Some pairings worked noticeably better than others. The fluid seemed to help certain men's sperm and not others. It even seemed to chemically attract some sperm over others (Fitzpatrick et al., 2020; Jokiniemi et al., 2020)ReferencesFitzpatrick, J. Studies in animals point in a similar direction (Løvlie et al., 2013)Full ReferenceLøvlie, H., Gillingham, M. A. F., Worley, K., Pizzari, T., & Richardson, D. S. (2013). The idea that the uterus and other female reproductive organs may "select" for the healthiest sperm is known as cryptic female choiceiCryptic Female ChoiceThe proposed ability of the female reproductive tract to favor some sperm over others after mating. Fertilization: Sperm Meets Egg

Compared to a sperm, the egg is enormous, and it doesn't sit there unprotected. It's wrapped in a tough, jelly-like shell called the zona pellucidaiZona PellucidaThe tough, glycoprotein "shell" surrounding the egg that a sperm must penetrate to fertilize it.View in glossary →, with a cloud of helper cells around it.

When a sperm reaches the egg, a small compartment on the sperm, known as the acrosome capiAcrosome CapThe enzyme-filled cap on the head of a sperm; it bursts to dissolve a path through the egg's outer coating.View in glossary →, bursts open, releasing enzymes that dissolve a tunnel through the jelly shell. The sperm wriggles through and, if successful, fuses with the egg.

The instant one sperm gets in, the egg reacts within seconds. It sets off a dramatic burst of zinc, nicknamed the "zinc spark," that hardens the outer shell so no other sperm can bind or break through (Duncan et al., 2016; Que et al., 2017)ReferencesDuncan, F. E., Que, E. L., Zhang, N., Feinberg, E. C., O'Halloran, T. V., & Woodruff, T. K. (2016). The zinc spark is an inorganic signature of human egg activation at fertilization. Scientific Reports, 6, 24737. | Que, E. L., Duncan, F. E., Bayer, A. R., Philips, S. J., Roth, E. W., Bleher, R., Gleber, S. C., Vogt, S., Woodruff, T. K., & O'Halloran, T. V. (2017). Zinc sparks induce physiochemical changes in the egg zona pellucida that prevent polyspermy. Integrative Biology, 9(2), 135–144.. This is to block the possibility of multiple sperm fertilizing the egg at once, a process known as polyspermyiPolyspermyFertilization of a single egg by more than one sperm. It produces too many chromosomes and is not viable, so the egg blocks it immediately.View in glossary →. The math has to be exact: one sperm, one egg. If multiple sperm fertilized the egg, the new cell would carry too many chromosomes and wouldn't survive.

Where does all of this happen? Not in the uterus, as many people assume, but out near the far end of the fallopian tube (Silverthorn, 2013)Full ReferenceSilverthorn, D. U. (2013). Human physiology: An integrated approach (6th ed.). Pearson Education.. The egg barely makes it away from the ovary before a sperm reaches it. The result is a single new cell carrying DNA from both partners, the first cell of a possible new person. From here it begins to divide and drift down toward the uterus, where it may eventually implant and begin the process of gestation.

Detecting a Pregnancy

For most people, the first hint that something has changed is a missed period. It is a classic sign of pregnancy, but it is not solid proof. Stress, illness, big swings in weight or exercise, and irregular cycles can all delay a period or skip one entirely. Early pregnancy tends to bring other clues too: tender or tingling breasts, more trips to the bathroom, fatigue, and nausea known as morning sickness. But even when all of these signs are present, it's important to take a pregnancy test to make sure.

Those few minutes waiting for a result are among the more emotionally loaded a person can have. The very same plus sign can land as the best news of someone's life or as a quiet catastrophe, depending entirely on whether the pregnancy is wanted. One person sees a future they have been hoping for; another sees school, work, money, or a relationship thrown into question. We carry that whole range into the rest of the chapter, because pregnancy is never only a biological event. It always arrives inside a particular life.

Fun Fact: The Original Pee Stick Was a Bag of Seeds. Long before drugstore strips, people improvised. An ancient Egyptian method, written on papyrus more than 3,000 years ago, had a woman urinate on bags of wheat and barley seeds for several days. If the grain sprouted, she was said to be pregnant. The likely reason is that pregnancy urine is loaded with hormones such as estrogen, which can nudge seeds to grow. The Egyptians were wrong that the grain could predict the baby's sex, but they had stumbled onto a fairly accurate way of testing for pregnancy.

7.2 Pregnancy and Prenatal Development

Pregnancy tends to get described as a single condition, the way we say someone "is pregnant" as though it were one steady state stretched across nine months. Living through it is closer to three distinct seasons, each with its own rapidly changing body inside and its own version of the person carrying it. Clinicians mark those seasons as the three trimestersiTrimesterOne of the three roughly three-month stages of pregnancy, each with distinct developmental milestones and bodily changes.View in glossary →, roughly three months apiece.

A pregnancy is dated from the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP)iLast Menstrual Period (LMP)The first day of the most recent period, used as the standard starting point for dating a pregnancy because it is easier to identify than the day of conception.View in glossary →, not from the day of conception. The reason is practical: most people can name the day their last period started, while very few know the exact day a sperm met an egg. This can create confusion when thinking about gestation, or how long a fertilized egg has been growing. If a person is "4 weeks pregnant," meaning it's been 4 weeks since their last period, they've likely only been gestating a fertilized egg for about 2 weeks. A full-term pregnancy takes about 40 weeks using this method, but if you discount the first 2 weeks, then a pregnancy is about 9 months long. We will be using the LMP method of counting pregnancy weeks throughout the textbook.

Nutrition and Prenatal Care

A pregnant body needs more of certain raw materials than usual, because it is building tissue, blood, and bone from scratch. Protein supplies the building blocks for new tissue. The fetus pulls iron from the blood circulating to the placenta, which can leave the pregnant person anemic and exhausted if iron intake is too low. Calcium goes into the baby's developing skeleton, and a shortage of this mineral is linked to high blood pressure in the parent. A good diet covers most of this, and a daily prenatal vitamin can help fill in the gaps.

The old instruction to "eat for two" is one of the stickier pregnancy myths. A fetus is tiny for most of the pregnancy and does not need a second adult's worth of food. For the entire first trimester, the recommended increase is essentially zero extra calories. Even in the final months of pregnancy, a pregnant person should only consume a few hundred extra calories a day, which is closer to a snack than a second dinner. What the body needs is not twice the quantity of food but better quality: more protein, iron, calcium, and folate.

The First Trimester (Weeks 1 to 13)

From One Cell to an Embryo

The single cell formed at fertilization does not stay single for long. As it drifts down the fallopian tube toward the uterus, it divides again and again, and within a few days it has become a hollow ball of cells called a blastocystiBlastocystThe hollow ball of cells that forms a few days after fertilization; its outer layer becomes the placenta and its inner cluster becomes the embryo.View in glossary →. Because so many major structures are being built in these first weeks, the first trimester is also the most vulnerable stretch of the whole pregnancy. A harmful exposure does the most damage when an organ is under construction, which is why doctors advise pregnant people to avoid certain substances during this time. We'll revisit this topic in another section.

The Support System

Three structures keep the fetus alive throughout a pregnancy. The placenta is the surface of the uterus, where the parent's blood and the fetus's blood run close enough to trade goods without ever mixing. Oxygen and nutrients cross from parent to fetus; carbon dioxide and waste cross back the other way. The placenta filters out many threats, so a parent can have a nasty cold while the fetus stays virus-free. However, the filter is not perfect. Some viruses and many drugs can slip across the barrier, which is the reason a pregnant person has to be careful about what enters their body. The placenta is also a hormone factory, producing the estrogen and progesterone that keep the pregnancy stable, plus the hCG that encourages rapid growth.

The umbilical cordiUmbilical CordThe flexible supply line carrying blood between the fetus and the placenta; its attachment point becomes the belly button after birth.View in glossary → is the supply line that runs between the fetus and the placenta, carrying blood back and forth through about twenty inches of flexible tubing. The spot where it attaches becomes the belly button after birth. The amniotic saciAmniotic SacThe fluid-filled membrane surrounding the fetus that cushions it and keeps its temperature stable.View in glossary → is like a padded room. The fetus floats inside it in warm amniotic fluidiAmniotic FluidThe warm liquid inside the amniotic sac that holds a steady temperature and cushions the fetus from outside jolts.View in glossary →, which holds a steady temperature and cushions every bump from the outside world. If the pregnant person trips and falls, the fluid absorbs the jolt and the fetus rides it out undisturbed. It is, in effect, the original waterbed.

What the Mother Feels

Most of the early symptoms of pregnancy trace back to a single cause: a flood of hormones. hCG climbs steeply in the first weeks, and estrogen and progesterone rise alongside it, first from the corpus luteum left behind at ovulation and then, by about week 12, from the placenta taking over the job.

The most famous of these symptoms is morning sicknessiMorning SicknessThe nausea and queasiness of early pregnancy. It can also worsen when a pregnant person is tired or running on an empty stomach. For most, it is a miserable but harmless experience, and fades by the second trimester. Fun Fact: Feeling Awful Might Be a Good Sign. Morning sickness seems pointless and cruel, but it may be doing a job. People who experience nausea and vomiting in early pregnancy are, on average, less likely to miscarry than those who sail through symptom-free (Flaxman & Sherman, 2000)Full ReferenceFlaxman, S. M., & Sherman, P. W. (2000). Morning sickness: A mechanism for protecting mother and embryo. Quarterly Review of Biology, 75(2), 113–148.. One leading explanation is that the queasiness is protective by design. It peaks exactly when the embryo's organs are forming and most fragile, and it pushes the pregnant person away from strong-tasting, bitter, or spoiled foods — the very things most likely to carry toxins or bacteria. A rough first trimester is the body playing it safe on the embryo's behalf.

Other early changes pile on: tender or tingling breasts, deep fatigue, frequent urination, food cravings, and sudden aversions to foods or smells that were fine a week earlier. Many of these overlap with ordinary premenstrual symptoms, which is exactly why the early signs alone cannot confirm a pregnancy.

The Couple's Experience

For expectant parents, the first trimester is often a tangle of opposite feelings. There are excitement and a sense of accomplishment, but also a sense of anxiety. People may be wary about all the bodily changes they're experiencing. New parents may also be anxious about the responsibility of caring for their first child. The first trimester carries the highest risk of miscarriage, which can also concern many couples. We'll be covering that topic in more detail later in the chapter.

There is also a built-in asymmetry that can strain a relationship. For the pregnant person, the pregnancy is concrete from day one, because their body is the thing changing. For a partner, it can stay abstract for weeks, an idea more than an experience. Eventually, an ultrasound image or a first kick can make it feel more real for them. That gap in felt reality is normal, but couples who do not see it coming sometimes read it as the partner not caring, when it is closer to the partner not yet "feeling" the pregnancy affect them.

The Second Trimester (Weeks 14 to 27)

If the first trimester is the rough patch, the second is often the reward. For most people the nausea lifts, energy comes back, and the other symptoms of pregnancy become much more manageable.

The Developing Fetus

The fetus grows fast now and starts doing recognizably human things. Sometime around weeks 16 to 20, the pregnant person feels the first flutters of movement, a milestone called quickeningiQuickeningThe first fetal movements a pregnant person can feel, usually between weeks 16 and 20.View in glossary →. First-time parents often notice it later than people who have been pregnant before, simply because they are not yet sure what they are feeling. By the middle of the trimester the fetus can hear, responds to light, and goes through cycles of rest and activity.

Around 20 weeks, an ultrasound, or sonogram, is typically performed to track growth and show the baby's position. The Couple's Experience

Emotionally, the second trimester tends to be the calm in the middle. The anxieties of the first trimester have eased, the worries about labor are not yet pressing, and the pregnancy feels more secure. It is often the point where a partner finally connects to the pregnancy in a felt way. Watching an ultrasound and placing a hand on the belly to feel the baby kick turns the abstract into something real. That shared, physical experience tends to draw couples closer, and a supportive partner during this stretch is one of the better predictors of lower anxiety as the pregnancy goes on.

The Third Trimester (Weeks 28 to 40)

The home stretch is the most physically taxing part of the journey. The fetus is finishing its preparations for life outside, and the pregnant person is carrying a great deal more than they were three months earlier.

The Developing Fetus

In these final months, the brain and nervous system mature rapidly. The lungs also do their last and most important fine-tuning, which is why even an extra week or two in the womb can meaningfully improve a newborn's odds of survival. The fetus is also packing on the fat that will help it regulate temperature after birth.

The Couple's Experience

Anticipation runs high in the last weeks, mixed with an eagerness to have the physical symptoms of pregnancy behind them. It is common for fresh anxieties to surface too: about the pain of labor, the baby's health, and about whether one is ready to be a parent at all. Many couples channel that energy into preparation, the nesting impulse to ready a home, take a birth class, and preplan for the new arrival.

7.3 Labor and Birth

Birth is two things at once. It is a physiological event, a sequence of muscular and hormonal changes the body runs largely on its own, and it is one of the most psychologically charged experiences a person can have. The biology is remarkably consistent from one person to the next. The experience is not. How someone experiences labor has a lot to do with whether they feel informed, supported, and in control throughout the process.

The Three Stages of Labor

For most of pregnancy the uterus stays quiet. What finally rouses it is a hormonal conversation between fetus and parent. In the weeks before birth, the fetus's adrenal glands release a surge of hormones that finishes maturing the lungs and signals to the parent that the fetus is ready for delivery. That signal prompts the parent's pituitary gland to release oxytociniOxytocinA pituitary hormone that triggers uterine contractions during labor and the milk let-down reflex during breastfeeding.View in glossary →, which triggers the uterus to make prostaglandinsiProstaglandinsHormone-like compounds that make the uterine muscles contract during labor.View in glossary →. Each contraction presses the baby's head against the cervix, and that pressure sends nerve signals back to the brain telling it to release even more oxytocin. More oxytocin means stronger contractions, which means more pressure, which means more oxytocin. It's a positive feedback loopiPositive Feedback LoopA self-amplifying process in which each step makes the next one bigger. In labor, contractions create pressure that releases more oxytocin, driving still stronger contractions.View in glossary →, a process that amplifies itself, each step making the next one bigger. 7.4 The Postpartum Period

While the pregnancy and birth tend to get all the attention, what comes after is just as crucial. The weeks after delivery, often called the fourth trimesteriFourth TrimesterThe first weeks after delivery, a demanding recovery period for both the new parent and the newborn.View in glossary →, require a new parent to recover from a major physical event, learn to keep a newborn alive, and reorganize their sense of self and their closest relationship. Not to mention this is a time when that person is likely getting very little sleep. The body's recovery is the most visible piece, but the psychological recovery is often much more difficult.

Postpartum Recovery

The body has just completed something close to an athletic feat, and it shows. For several days to a few weeks, the uterus sheds its pregnancy lining as a bloody discharge called lochiaiLochiaThe bloody vaginal discharge that occurs for days to weeks after birth as the uterus sheds its pregnancy lining.View in glossary →. Tears and other physical damage to the vagina also take time to heal, making sitting, walking, and using the bathroom uncomfortable for a while.

During pregnancy, the placenta pumps out high levels of estrogen and progesterone. After delivery, these hormones crash within days. That sudden drop causes many psychological effects, such as mood swings, irritability, and sometimes depression. Recovery post-pregnancy should be measured in months, not in the 2 weeks that a short maternity leave often allows for. The idea that people should quickly "bounce back" after a pregnancy only adds to the sense of guilt and shame that many parents experience when they aren't faring well months after giving birth, even though that's quite normal.

The Bonding Myth

Few ideas about new parenthood have caused as much needless guilt as the bondingiBondingThe deep, lasting attachment between parent and child. In the 1970s, pediatricians Marshall Klaus and John Kennell proposed that the hours right after birth were a "sensitive period," a brief window in which mother and infant had to make skin-to-skin contact in order to bond properly, much like the imprinting seen in some animals (Klaus & Kennell, 1976)Full ReferenceKlaus, M. It also turned out to be wrong. Later research found little evidence for a make-or-break bonding window (Eyer, 1992)Full ReferenceEyer, D. E. (1992). Mother-infant bonding: A scientific fiction. Parents who deliver by cesarean under general anesthesia, parents whose newborns go straight to intensive care, and adoptive parents who meet their children days or years later all form deep and lasting bonds. If a closing window existed, none of that would be possible.

This myth subtly puts blame on parents who don't instantly feel bonded with their newborn. A bond between parent and child is not sparked during one golden hour. It is built over months of feeding, soothing, and responding to an infant. It is a slow process rather than a single moment. However, skin-to-skin contact right after birth still has its advantages. Studies show it helps keep the newborn warm, steadies their heart rate, and gets breastfeeding started sooner, which is why hospitals still encourage it. The correction is only to the stakes: early contact is a good beginning, not a make-or-break moment for bonding.

Sex After a Baby

A baby changes a couple's sex life, often more than new parents expect. Clinicians generally advise waiting until around the six-week checkup before resuming penetrative sex, to let any tearing heal and lower the risk of infection. Although, other forms of intimacy, such as oral or manual sex, present no harm to new moms. When penetrative sex does resume, it can be uncomfortable or painful at first, whether from healing perineal tissue, a cesarean incision, or the vaginal dryness that breastfeeding and drops in estrogen can produce.

Studies of the transition to parenthood find that the great majority of new parents, more than 90 percent, report sexual concerns they did not have before. The couples who come through this best are those who talk openly about these changes, keep their expectations realistic, and treat the dip as a temporary blip in their normal sex lives.

Feeding the Baby

Feeding is where much of the postpartum pressure concentrates. While the biology is relatively simple, it can be a thorny issue in many families. It doesn't help that modern culture places a lot of loaded feelings and assumptions onto breastfeeding, so it can often feel like you're not doing it "right" no matter what you do.

The benefits are real, but newborns placed on formula instead of breast milk aren't at a major disadvantage. Differences between newborns raised on breastmilk vs formula tend to be short-term and are mostly related to the immune system. Breastfed infants tend to have fewer ear, respiratory, and gastrointestinal infections. Breastfeeding parents also see benefits, with reduced rates of breast and ovarian cancer and type 2 diabetes. The longer-term claims about IQ, obesity, and chronic disease are far murkier, because breastfeeding is more common in families with higher levels of education and income. In the United States, more than 80% of parents start out breastfeeding, but only about 25% are still breastfeeding exclusively at six months. The most common reasons for stopping are practical: trouble with latching or milk supply, pain, and the nearly impossible prospect of feeding an infant while working a full-time job. Treating breastfeeding as a test of maternal devotion adds guilt into the mix. The AAP itself, in its 2022 update, paired its recommendation for breastfeeding with a call for nonjudgmental support, acknowledging that exclusive breastfeeding is not possible for every family and that babies thrive on breast milk, formula, or a combination. A well-fed baby and a supportive community are the goals that matter.

7.5 When Pregnancy Is Complicated

Most pregnancies are uneventful and end with a healthy baby. A meaningful minority, though, run into complications that range from the merely worrying to the life-threatening. While some of these complications are unavoidable, many can be deterred by knowing the danger signs and catching things early.

Miscarriage

MiscarriageiMiscarriageThe loss of a pregnancy before the fetus can survive on its own. But the vast majority of miscarriages aren't due to anything the parent does or doesn't do. The pregnancy simply wasn't meant to be.

Ectopic pregnancies occur in roughly 1–2% of pregnancies, often when scarring has blocked the tube and trapped the fertilized egg on its way to the uterus. An ectopic pregnancy cannot produce a baby, and it is dangerous to the pregnant person. As the embryo grows, it can rupture the tube and cause severe internal bleeding. Ectopic pregnancy is one of the leading causes of maternal death in the first trimester. The warning signs are sharp or one-sided abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and sometimes shoulder pain. If someone shows these signs, especially for more than 1 day, they should seek emergency care. Caught early, an ectopic pregnancy can be ended with medication or surgery before it ruptures.

The delivery itself, beginning when the cervix is fully open and the baby descends through the birth canal until it is born.
Teratogen
Any substance that crosses into the developing fetus and disrupts its development. Timing matters: the same exposure is far more harmful while organs are forming.
Thermotaxis
Movement guided by temperature; sperm sense the slightly warmer region near the egg and steer toward the heat.
Third Stage of Labor
The delivery of the afterbirth, when a few final contractions detach and expel the placenta after the baby is born.
Transition
The short, most intense phase at the end of the first stage of labor, when contractions are strongest and closest together.
Trimester
One of the three roughly three-month stages of pregnancy, each with distinct developmental milestones and bodily changes.
Umbilical Cord
The flexible supply line carrying blood between the fetus and the placenta; its attachment point becomes the belly button after birth.
Vacuum Extractor
A device that applies gentle suction to the baby's scalp to help guide it out during an assisted vaginal delivery.
VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean)
An attempt to deliver vaginally after a previous cesarean. Around 20–40% are unsuccessful and end in an emergency C-section.
Viability
The point at which a fetus could survive outside the womb, currently around 22 to 24 weeks, with survival rising steeply each added week.
Zona Pellucida
The tough, glycoprotein "shell" surrounding the egg that a sperm must penetrate to fertilize it.
