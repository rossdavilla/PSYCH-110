4.1 Understanding Sex and Gender

In May 2011, a couple in Toronto named Kathy Witterick and David Stocker made an announcement that became international news. They had just had their third child, named Storm, and they had decided not to tell anyone whether Storm was a boy or a girl. Not their friends. Not the grandparents. Not even Storm's two older siblings. They wanted to give Storm the chance to grow up without the strict expectations that come with being labeled at birth (Poisson, 2011)ReferencePoisson, J. (2011, May 21). Parents keep child's gender secret. Toronto Star..

Reactions were intense. Some people praised the parents for giving their child freedom. Others worried that Storm would be confused or bullied. The case raised a question that this chapter will return to again and again: how much of what we call "boy" and "girl" is built into our bodies, and how much is shaped by the world we grow up in?

To answer that, we need to be clear about our terms. People often use the words sex and gender as if they mean the same thing. They are actually two distinct concepts.

When a baby is born and the doctor announces, "It's a girl," that announcement is based on the appearance of the baby's external genitals. Sex is mostly determined by biology, but as you will see later in this chapter, sex is not always neatly male or female.

Gender identity is psychological. For most people, gender identity matches the sex they were assigned at birth. For some people, it does not. People whose gender identity does not match their assigned sex may describe themselves with the umbrella term transgender. We will return to this topic in detail in a later chapter.

Some forms of gender expression are coded as masculine, others as feminine, others as androgynous, meaning a blend between the two. A person whose gender expression matches what society expects for their gender usually attracts little notice. A person whose gender expression breaks those expectations, such as a man in a dress or a woman with a shaved head, may attract a great deal of notice and perhaps even stigma.

So what happened to Storm? Kathy Witterick and David Stocker continued to receive letters, criticism, and the occasional shouted insult on the sidewalk, but they held to their decision. In a 2016 follow-up interview with the Toronto Star, the family shared that Storm, who was by then 5-years-old, was using "she" pronouns and identified as a girl. Her older sibling Jazz, who had been assigned male at birth, had also begun using "she" and had taken to wearing dresses and growing her hair long. Their parents had not assigned a gender at birth; their children, on their own timelines, had each arrived at one.

In the language of this section, Storm's sex at birth had been kept private, but her gender identity — her internal sense of who she was — emerged as she grew, just as it does for any child. Her gender expression, the dresses and the chosen pronouns, was hers to figure out rather than handed to her. The Stocker-Witterick parents had not erased gender from their children's lives. They had simply refused to treat it as something adults assign and children obey. As it turned out, Storm did come to identify within the two familiar categories. Jazz, despite being assigned male at birth, did not.

The case does not prove that gender is purely social, or purely biological, or that any one approach to parenting is better than another. What it does show is that while gender and sex are complex concepts, kids can still find a way to figure them out for themselves.

The Gender Binary and the Gender Spectrum

Western culture tends to treat sex and gender as a binaryibinaryA way of thinking that divides something into exactly two opposing categories with nothing in between. Sex and gender are often treated as binary in Western culture.View in glossary →, meaning a set of only two options: male or female, man or woman, with nothing in between. This view is so familiar that it can be hard to step back and notice. Almost every form you fill out, from a driver's license application to an account on a social media app, asks you to pick one or the other. When going to the bathroom in public, you typically only have two options: Men's room or women's room. Children who sign up to play sports are usually only given two team options: the boys' team or girls' team.

A more inclusive way to think about sex and gender is as a spectrumispectrumA range of possibilities with many valid points along it, often used as an alternative to the binary view of sex and gender. A regular light switch has only two settings: on and off. A dimmer can sit anywhere on the dial, from very bright to very dim, with countless settings in between. For many people, gender works more like a dimmer than a binary switch (Diamond, 2002; Newman, 2002)ReferenceDiamond, M. (2002). Plenty of people sit at the far ends of the dial. Plenty of others sit somewhere in the middle, and there is no good reason their settings are any less real or valid.

4.2 Prenatal Sex Differentiation

Every human being starts out the same way: as a single cell. Before that cell becomes a baby that anyone would describe as male or female, it goes through a long process called prenatal sex differentiationiprenatal sex differentiationThe developmental process by which a single fertilized egg becomes a body that is recognizably male, female, or somewhere in between.View in glossary →. The process is delicate, complicated, and easy to disrupt. By the time a baby is born, the result is usually (but not always) a body that looks clearly male or clearly female. But the path from a single cell to that result is anything but simple.

The Starting Point: Undifferentiated Sex

At conception, the future human consists of one fertilized egg cell. That cell contains 23 pairs of chromosomes, including one pair that determines biological sex. People who develop into typical females usually have two X chromosomes (XX). People who develop into typical males usually have one X chromosome and one Y chromosome (XY).

Although X and Y are both sex chromosomes, they are very different in size. The X chromosome carries about 1,090 genes, while the Y chromosome only carries about 80 (Federman, 2006)ReferenceFederman, D. D. (2006). The biology of human sex differences. New England Journal of Medicine, 354(14), 1507–1514.. The Y is one of the smallest chromosomes in the human body. Most of the genetic instructions for building a human come from the other 22 pairs of chromosomes and from the X. The Y mostly does just one job: nudge a developing embryo toward becoming male.

For the first six weeks after conception, you cannot tell an XX embryo and an XY embryo apart. They look identical. They can become either, depending on whether the SRY gene is active.View in glossary →, meaning gonads that could become either ovaries or testes. Both have two sets of internal ducts: the Müllerian ductsiMüllerian ductsA pair of ducts in the early embryo that develop into the female internal reproductive tract (fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, and upper vagina). In females, they break down and disappear.View in glossary →. And both have the same simple set of external structures: a small bump called the genital tubercleigenital tubercleA small bump of tissue in the early embryo that becomes the glans of the penis in males or the glans of the clitoris in females.View in glossary →, two urethral foldsiurethral foldsTwo folds of tissue on either side of the genital tubercle in the early embryo. At this point, biological sex has been determined by the chromosomes the embryo carries, but the body has not started to show it.

Fun Fact: Mom Does Not Pick the Baby's Sex

A long-standing myth says that the mother determines whether her baby is a boy or a girl. In humans, this is biologically backwards. The mother's eggs always carry an X chromosome. The father's sperm carries either an X or a Y. So it is actually the sperm, and therefore the biological father, that determines the baby's chromosomal sex.

The Reproductive Tract

At about seven weeks after conception, the chromosomes start sending instructions. Once the testes form, they start producing two important hormones. The first is anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)ianti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)A hormone produced by the developing testes that causes the Müllerian ducts to break down and disappear, preventing development of the female internal reproductive tract.View in glossary →, which causes the Müllerian ducts to break down and disappear (Vilain, 2000)ReferenceVilain, E. In an XX embryo, the opposite happens. Without AMH, the Müllerian ducts develop into the female internal reproductive tract: the fallopian tubes (also called the oviducts), the uterus, the cervix, and the upper part of the vagina. Without testosterone, the Wolffian ducts break down and disappear.

It is worth pausing on what this means. Female development is not a separate path from male development. It is the default path that happens when the male signals do not turn on. If you remove the testes from a male embryo before they start producing hormones, that embryo will develop a female body, even though it has XY chromosomes.

Developing the Genitalia

At about four weeks after conception, the external genitals of all embryos look the same: a small bump (the genital tubercle), two folds of tissue surrounding a slit, and two swellings on either side. Whether these structures become a penis and scrotum or a clitoris and labia depends on whether testosterone is present during the next several weeks (Vilain, 2000)ReferenceVilain, E. (2000). Genetics of sexual development. Annual Review of Sex Research, 11(1), 1–25..

In all embryos, the gonads start out high in the abdominal cavity, near the kidneys. In females, the ovaries drift down slightly after birth and settle into their adult position on either side of the uterus, inside the pelvis. In males, the testes have a much longer trip to make. Shortly before birth, the testes settle into the scrotum, and the inguinal canal closes off.

About 3 percent of male babies are born with one or both testes still in the abdomen (Schneuer et al., 2018)ReferenceSchneuer, F. J., et al. (2018). If they have not dropped by age one, doctors can fix the problem with a simple surgery. If the testes are left inside the abdomen long-term, the warmer temperature inside the body interferes with sperm production, and the man will be infertile.

Homologous and Analogous Organs

Looking at adult male and adult female bodies, the genitals appear to be completely different from one another, but they're actually quite similar. Every structure of the female reproductive system has a male counterpart that developed from the same starting tissue in the embryo. 4.3 Puberty as a Second Differentiation

The first big wave of sex differentiation happens before you are born. The second happens 9–12 years later, at the start of pubertyipubertyThe developmental period during which a child's body transforms into an adult body capable of sexual reproduction. It typically begins between ages 9 and 11 in girls and between ages 10 and 12 in boys.View in glossary →. This is the period during which a child's body transforms into an adult body capable of sexual reproduction. It is also a period during which many of us have personal memories of confusion, embarrassment, and self-consciousness. Puberty is, in many ways, the most awkward biological event most people will ever live through.

After birth, sex hormone levels in both boys and girls drop sharply and stay low for the next decade. The body simply does not need them yet. Sometime between ages 9 and 11 in girls, and between ages 10 and 12 in boys, the system flips back on (Tanner, 1967)ReferenceTanner, J. M. (1967). Growth at adolescence (2nd ed.). Blackwell Scientific..

Changes in Boys

The first noticeable sign of puberty in boys is the growth of the testes and scrotum, usually around ages 9 to 10. Pubic hair starts growing around the same time. About a year later, the penis begins to enlarge, first in width and then in length. By ages 13 to 14, most boys are capable of ejaculation, though the first ejaculate does not yet contain mature sperm. By around age 15, the ejaculate contains mature sperm, and the boy is biologically capable of fathering a child.

Several other changes happen at the same time. Facial hair begins to appear, usually starting with fuzz on the upper lip. The voice changes as the larynx grows, sometimes producing the embarrassing cracks and squeaks that are a comedy staple of films about middle school. A growth spurt occurs between ages 11 and 16, adding inches of height and pounds of muscle. Sweat glands and oil glands become more active, often producing acne. Erections become more frequent, sometimes at inconvenient moments, and many boys begin having nocturnal emissions, also called wet dreams. Almost all of these changes are driven by testosterone.

Changes in Girls

The first noticeable sign of puberty in girls is breast development, usually beginning around ages 9 to 10. Pubic hair appears shortly after. The hips and buttocks grow more rounded as fat is added in those areas, and the pelvic bones widen, creating a passageway large enough for a baby's head to fit through during childbirth.

The most identifiable single event of female puberty is the first menstrual period, called menarcheimenarcheA girl's first menstrual period, marking the beginning of fertility. In the United States, menarche occurs on average around age 12.5.View in glossary →. In Canada, the average age of menarche is 12.7 years (Al-Sahab et al., 2010)ReferenceAl-Sahab, B., Ardern, C. I., Hamadeh, M. J., & Tamim, H. (2010). Age at menarche in Canada: Results from the National Longitudinal Survey of Children & Youth. BMC Public Health, 10, 736.. In the United States, the average is slightly younger. About 15 percent of girls have their first period before age 11.5; most have it between 11.5 and 13.9; about 17 percent have it after 13.9. There is no one "right" age.

What determines when menarche happens? The leading explanation is the percent body fat hypothesisipercent body fat hypothesisFrisch and McArthur's (1974) proposal that menarche cannot begin until a girl's body has stored enough fat (roughly 17 to 22 percent) to support a possible pregnancy.View in glossary →. A girl whose body is too thin to feed a baby would not benefit from being fertile, so her body delays fertility until she is large enough. This hypothesis also helps explain why menstruation can stop in women who are extremely thin, including women with anorexia and women who train as elite distance runners. When body fat falls below about 17 to 22 percent, the system that maintains the menstrual cycle shuts down.

Fun Fact: Puberty Is Showing Up Earlier

The average age of puberty has been dropping for decades. In 1860, the average age of a girl's first period in Western countries was about 16. Today in the United States, it is about 12.5 (Posner, 2006)ReferencePosner, R. B. (2006). Improved nutrition explains some of this, since a critical amount of body fat is needed for puberty to begin. But two more recent factors also seem to play a role: rising rates of childhood obesity, and exposure to endocrine disruptors — chemicals in plastics, pesticides, and personal care products that mimic the effects of estrogen (Mouritsen et al., 2010; Eckert-Lind et al., 2020)ReferenceMouritsen, A., et al. (2010). Hypothesis: Exposure to endocrine-disrupting chemicals may interfere with timing of puberty. International Journal of Andrology, 33(2), 346–359. | Eckert-Lind, C., et al. (2020). One indicator that pollution plays a role is that girls raised in urban environments, with more exposure to toxins, tend to experience menarche earlier than girls raised in rural (cleaner) environments.

Precocious and Delayed Puberty

Not every child enters puberty at the average age. Precocious pubertyiprecocious pubertyPuberty that begins unusually early, before age 8 in girls or age 9 in boys. Precocious puberty has been on the rise. The leading suspects are rising rates of childhood obesity (since fat tissue produces both estrogen and leptin) and increasing exposure to endocrine disruptors in the environment. The main risk is that an early growth spurt may end growth earlier as well, so a child who enters puberty very young may not grow as tall as they otherwise would have. Early puberty in girls has also been linked to higher rates of depression and anxiety, in part because their bodies look adult before they are emotionally prepared to deal with how the world responds.

Delayed puberty is usually caused by chronic illness, malnourishment, or a hormonal problem. In most cases it can be treated. Children with delayed puberty who are otherwise healthy often end up taller than average because they have more years to grow before their bones finish.

4.4 Intersex Variations

So far, this chapter has described sex development as if it always goes one of two ways: XX or XY, ovaries or testes, female or male. For most people, that is roughly how it works. But sex development is a long, complicated process involving dozens of genes, multiple hormones, and several developmental windows. With that many moving parts, things sometimes go in a less typical direction. People whose bodies do not match either of the two standard developmental patterns are described as intersexiintersexA person born with reproductive or sexual anatomy that does not fit the typical male or female patterns. Estimates of prevalence range from about 1 in 1,000 births to roughly 1 percent, depending on which conditions are counted.View in glossary →.

What Does Intersex Mean?

An intersex person is born with a reproductive or sexual anatomy that does not fit the usual male or female patterns. There are many specific intersex conditions. Some are caused by atypical chromosomes; others by atypical hormone responses; others by physical developmental differences with no clear cause. Some are visible at birth, while others are not discovered until puberty or even later.

Estimates of how common intersex conditions are vary. Fun Fact: The Third Gender of the Dominican Republic

In a small village called Las Salinas in the Dominican Republic, 5-ARD is unusually common, affecting roughly 1 in 90 people (Imperato-McGinley et al., 1974)ReferenceImperato-McGinley, J., Guerrero, L., Gautier, T., & Peterson, R. Reality Check: The Case of David Reimer

In 1966, a Canadian couple named Janet and Ron Reimer brought their identical twin sons, Bruce and Brian, in for a routine circumcision. Something went terribly wrong, and Bruce's penis was destroyed. The doctors had no way to reconstruct it.

A few months later, Janet Reimer saw a television interview with Dr. John Money, a psychologist at Johns Hopkins University who specialized in cases of unusual sex development. Money believed gender identity was almost entirely a product of how a child was raised, not biology. He thought that with surgery and hormone treatments, a child born male could be raised successfully as a girl, as long as the change happened early enough.

The Reimers consulted Dr. Money. At about 22 months of age, Bruce had his testicles removed and was renamed Brenda. From that point on, his parents raised him as a girl. He was given estrogen at puberty. Dr. Money wrote scientific papers and gave public talks about the case for years, describing it as a success and using it as evidence that gender was learned rather than biological.

The case was not a success.

Brenda hated dresses. She refused dolls. She preferred climbing trees and rough play with her brother. She was teased relentlessly by other children for her masculine mannerisms. By adolescence, she was deeply depressed and suicidal. When her parents finally told her the truth, at age 14, against Dr. Money's advice, her reaction was relief. She said, in essence, that explains everything.

She immediately changed her name to David, cut her hair, and began the process of returning to living as a man. He underwent a double mastectomy to remove the breasts that had grown from estrogen treatment. He took testosterone. He met a woman, married, and helped raise her three children. In 2004, at the age of 38, after years of depression, the death of his twin brother, and serious financial setbacks, David Reimer died by suicide.

David's story is heartbreaking. It also taught the medical community a lasting lesson: gender identity is not infinitely flexible. It is not something parents and doctors can simply assign by raising a child a certain way. Trying to force a person into a gender that does not match their identity causes lasting harm.

Current Best Practices

David Reimer was not born intersex. But for decades, his case was used to justify a standard medical approach to intersex babies that was almost identical to what happened to him: doctors would surgically modify the baby's genitals to look more clearly male or female, often within the first few weeks of life. Parents were often advised to raise the child in the assigned gender and not to discuss what had been done.

That approach is no longer considered ethical. Intersex adults who underwent these surgeries as infants have spoken out, often describing themselves as having been physically mutilated and assigned to a gender that did not match how they came to identify. Studies of long-term outcomes have shown that early surgeries often produce poor results, both physically (impaired sexual sensation, scarring, repeated surgeries) and psychologically (Creighton & Minto, 2001; Minto et al., 2003)ReferenceCreighton, S. M., & Minto, C. L. (2001). Managing intersex: Most vaginal surgery in childhood should be deferred. BMJ, 323(7324), 1264–1265. | Minto, C. L., et al. (2003). The effect of clitoral surgery on sexual outcome in individuals who have intersex conditions with ambiguous genitalia. The Lancet, 361(9365), 1252–1257..

The current recommendation, supported by the American Academy of Pediatrics and the United Nations among others, is that doctors should not perform medically unnecessary genital surgeries on intersex children too young to consent (Lee et al., 2006)ReferenceLee, P. A., Houk, C. P., Ahmed, S. F., & Hughes, I. A. (2006). Consensus statement on management of intersex disorders. Pediatrics, 118(2), e488–e500.. Parents are encouraged to assign a tentative gender at birth, or no gender at all, and to educate their child about their body as they grow. When the child develops a clear gender identity, they can decide for themselves what, if any, medical interventions they want.

The shift in approach reflects a deeper shift in thinking. Intersex people are not broken versions of male or female. They are simply another point on the spectrum of human sex development.

4.5 Gender Differences and Similarities

If you scroll through any pop psychology section in a bookstore, you will find titles claiming that men and women come from different planets, that men want sex and women want love, that men are visual and women are emotional, that men are better at math while women are better at expressing their feelings. The list goes on. These claims sell books, but they are mostly outright wrong or heavily exaggerated.

In 2005, the psychologist Janet Hyde reviewed decades of research comparing men and women on dozens of psychological traits and abilities. Her conclusion became known as the gender similarities hypothesisigender similarities hypothesisJanet Hyde's (2005) claim, based on decades of research, that men and women are far more alike than different on most psychological traits and abilities.View in glossary →: for most psychological variables, men and women are far more alike than different (Hyde, 2005)ReferenceHyde, J. S. (2005). The gender similarities hypothesis. American Psychologist, 60(6), 581–592.. Where differences exist, they are usually small, and there is enormous overlap between the male and female distributions. If you took a random man and a random woman and tried to guess which was which based on almost any single psychological trait, you would be wrong more often than you would be right.

That said, some gender differences are real and meaningful. The challenge is figuring out which ones.

The Sexual Double Standard

Possibly the most damaging gender difference in sexuality is not in behavior at all but in how that behavior is judged. Research consistently finds that men and women face very different social reactions for the same sexual choices (England & Bearak, 2014)ReferenceEngland, P., & Bearak, J. (2014). The English language has dozens of insulting words for sexually active women — slut, whore, hoe — and historically very few for men. The recent appearance of words like fuckboy shows some change, but the imbalance remains.

This is called the sexual double standardisexual double standardThe pattern by which men and women face different social judgments for the same sexual behavior. It shapes how people behave, how they report their behavior, and how they think about themselves. Women may downplay their sexual experience to avoid judgment. Men may exaggerate theirs to gain status. Both biases muddle the data on how men and women actually differ.

Reporting Biases

One of the trickiest problems in studying gender differences is that people lie. Not maliciously, usually — but in subtle ways that bring their answers more in line with what they think they should say.

A clever study by Alexander and Fisher (2003)ReferenceAlexander, M. G., & Fisher, T. D. (2003). Truth and consequences: Using the bogus pipeline to examine sex differences in self-reported sexuality. In the first, participants believed their answers were completely anonymous. In the second, they felt their answers might be seen by the experimenter. When they thought they could lie freely, men and women reported very different numbers of sexual partners and very different attitudes. When they thought a lie detector would catch them, those differences shrank considerably.

This finding is important. It suggests that some of the gender differences we see in research are real, but some are just people performing the role they think they are supposed to play. Whenever you read a study claiming a big gender difference in sexual behavior, it is worth asking how the data were collected.

4.6 Where Do Gender Differences Come From?

If men and women differ in some ways — and they do, even if not as much as stereotypes suggest — where do those differences come from? Theorists have offered several explanations. None is fully correct on its own. The most useful way to think about this is that biological, psychological, and cultural forces are all working at the same time, and the truth involves some of each.

The Biological Perspective

The biological perspective argues that some gender differences are rooted in body chemistry — particularly prenatal hormones — and would exist even without any social input.

One key piece of evidence comes from girls with CAH, who as you read earlier are exposed to unusually high levels of androgens before birth. These girls are more likely to prefer stereotypically "boy" toys, more likely to engage in rough-and-tumble play, and as adults more likely to choose traditionally male-dominated careers and hobbies (Berenbaum & Hines, 1992)ReferenceBerenbaum, S. A., & Hines, M. (1992). Early androgens are related to childhood sex-typed toy preferences. Psychological Science, 3(3), 203–206.. This is hard to explain through socialization alone, since CAH girls are usually raised the same way as other girls.

A second piece of evidence comes from studies of typical pregnancies in which researchers measured the amount of testosterone in the amniotic fluid surrounding the developing fetus. Higher prenatal testosterone correlated with stronger preference for stereotypically masculine toys after birth, regardless of the child's chromosomal sex (Auyeung et al., 2009; van de Beek et al., 2009)ReferenceAuyeung, B., et al. (2009). Fetal testosterone predicts sexually differentiated childhood behavior in girls and in boys. Psychological Science, 20(2), 144–148. | van de Beek, C., et al. (2009). Prenatal sex hormones (maternal and amniotic fluid) and gender-related play behavior in 13-month-old infants. Archives of Sexual Behavior, 38(1), 6–15..

The ratio is laid down before birth and is influenced by prenatal testosterone exposure. People exposed to more prenatal testosterone tend to have a lower ratio, meaning their index and ring fingers are more similar in length. On average, men have lower 2D:4D ratios than women, but the ratio varies a lot within each sex. It is not a perfect measure, but it is one of the only ways researchers have to look back at a person's prenatal hormone exposure long after the fact.

Evolutionary Psychology

The evolutionary psychology perspective argues that male and female bodies and minds were shaped by millions of years of different reproductive challenges, and that some gender differences are leftover adaptations to those challenges.

The most famous statement of this view comes from David Buss (1989)ReferenceBuss, D. M. (1989). Sex differences in human mate preferences: Evolutionary hypotheses tested in 37 cultures. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 12(1), 1–14., who surveyed more than 10,000 people in 37 cultures. He found consistent cross-cultural patterns in what men and women look for in a partner. Men, on average, tended to place more emphasis on youth and physical attractiveness — traits associated with fertility. Women, on average, tended to place more emphasis on financial stability and social status — traits associated with the ability to provide for children. Men were also more likely to feel jealous about a partner's sexual infidelity; women were more likely to feel jealous about a partner's emotional infidelity.

Evolutionary psychologists explain these patterns through parental investment theoryiparental investment theoryAn evolutionary theory (Trivers, 1972) proposing that the sex which invests more biological resources in offspring should be choosier about mates, while the sex which invests less should be more open to short-term mating.View in glossary → (Trivers, 1972)ReferenceTrivers, R. L. (1972). Parental investment and sexual selection. In B. Campbell (Ed.), Sexual selection and the descent of man, 1871–1971 (pp. 136–179). Aldine.. A female mammal commits an enormous amount of biological investment to each pregnancy: nine months of carrying the fetus, the risks of childbirth, years of nursing. A male mammal's minimum biological investment is much smaller. The theory predicts that, on average, females should be choosier about partners and males should be more open to short-term mating opportunities, because the cost of a "bad" partner is much higher for the female.

Critics of evolutionary psychology argue that the theory is too simple and too easy to use to justify whatever pattern happens to be observed in the modern world (Geertz, 1973)ReferenceGeertz, C. (1973). The interpretation of cultures: Selected essays. Basic Books.. They point out that genes code for proteins, not for complex behaviors like "fall in love with high-status partners." Many of the patterns Buss documented can also be explained by the simple fact that, in most societies he studied, women had less access to resources of their own, and so they naturally placed more emphasis on a partner's ability to provide them.

Fun Fact: Vervet Monkeys and Their Toys

In a now-famous study, researchers gave a group of vervet monkeys a selection of toys typically marketed to children: trucks, dolls, books, and stuffed animals. The male monkeys spent more time with the trucks. The female monkeys spent more time with the dolls. Neither group had ever been to a toy store or seen a toy commercial.

This does not mean that human toy preferences are purely biological, since the human social environment is much more powerful than anything a monkey experiences. But it is one piece of evidence that prenatal hormones may shape preferences before culture has a chance to weigh in.

Social Learning Theory

The social learningisocial learningA theory associated with Albert Bandura that most gender differences are learned through reward, punishment, and imitation rather than being built in from birth.View in glossary → perspective, associated with psychologist Albert Bandura, argues that most gender differences are learned through reward, punishment, and imitation (Bandura, 1971)ReferenceBandura, A. (1971). Social learning theory. General Learning Press..

From the moment of birth, parents, relatives, teachers, peers, and the media treat boys and girls differently. Boys are often given trucks and action figures; girls are often given dolls and play kitchens. Boys are praised for being tough and competitive; girls are praised for being kind and pretty. Boys are scolded for crying; girls are scolded for being aggressive. These messages add up over thousands of small interactions every year.

Children also learn by watching. A girl who sees her mother handle most of the cooking and laundry, and her father handle most of the bill-paying and lawn care, is absorbing a lesson about what men and women do, even if no one ever sits her down and teaches it to her. Television, movies, music videos, and social media all reinforce these patterns. By the time a child enters school, they have already absorbed a great deal of information about what is expected of "boys" and "girls."

The strength of the social learning perspective is that it explains why gender norms vary so much across cultures and across time. A century ago in the United States, pink was considered a boy's color and blue was considered a girl's color. Today, the opposite is true. Whatever is "biologically natural" about color preference clearly cannot explain a switch like that.

The Cognitive Perspective

Children do not just passively absorb messages about gender. They actively build their own understanding of what gender means and use it to interpret the world.

Drawing on the work of Jean Piaget (1951)ReferencePiaget, J. (1951). Play, dreams and imitation in childhood. Routledge., psychologists studying gender development have identified rough stages children pass through. At birth, a child has no concept of gender. Between ages two and five, most children develop a strong, often rigid sense of gender categories: "boys have penises and girls have vaginas; boys play with trucks and girls play with dolls." A child this age may insist that a man with long hair is really a woman, or that a girl wearing pants is really a boy.

As children's brains mature, they become capable of more flexible thinking. Many children come to understand that gender is more nuanced — that the rules are mostly arbitrary, that a girl can like trucks without being a boy, and that not everything has to fit in a box. Other children continue to hold rigid views, often because the pressure to fit in at school reinforces them.

Putting It All Together

So which theory is right? In some sense, all of them. Sex hormones really do affect the developing brain. Evolutionary pressures really did shape some aspects of human mating psychology. Children really do learn from rewards, punishments, and imitation. And children really do build mental schemas that influence how they interpret the world.

The mistake is to treat any one of these theories as the whole story. Gender differences are not purely biological. They are not purely cultural. They are the product of biology and culture interacting from the moment of conception forward. The best researchers in the field do not pick a single perspective and stick with it; they look at the question in front of them and ask which factors are most likely to be doing the work.

That, in the end, is the most useful idea this chapter can give you. The next time someone tells you that "men are just like that" or "women are just like that," whatever the topic, you now have the tools to ask better questions. Is the difference real, or just stereotype? If it is real, how big is it? Where might it come from? And, perhaps most importantly, what would change if the circumstances of people's lives changed?

Key Terms

2D:4D digit ratio
The ratio of the index (second) finger to the ring (fourth) finger, set before birth and influenced by prenatal testosterone exposure. On average, men have lower 2D:4D ratios than women.
5-Alpha Reductase Deficiency (5-ARD)
An intersex condition in which an XY person lacks the enzyme needed to convert testosterone into dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the hormone that masculinizes external genitals before birth. Babies are often born with female-appearing or ambiguous genitals; at puberty, the surge of testosterone produces more typically male changes.
Analogous organs
Body parts that perform similar functions despite developing from different embryonic tissue.
Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (AIS)
An intersex condition in which an XY person produces normal testosterone but the body's cells cannot respond to it, causing the body to develop along the female pathway. AIS is usually not detected until adolescence, when the expected first period does not happen.
Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)
A hormone produced by the developing testes that causes the Müllerian ducts to break down and disappear, preventing development of the female internal reproductive tract.
Binary
A way of thinking that divides something into exactly two opposing categories with nothing in between. Sex and gender are often treated as binary in Western culture.
Bipotential gonads
The early reproductive organs in an embryo, before they differentiate into either testes or ovaries. They can become either, depending on whether the SRY gene is active.
Bogus pipeline
A research technique in which participants are told they are connected to a lie detector, even though the device is fake. The belief that lies may be caught leads to more honest reporting.
Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)
An intersex condition in which the adrenal glands produce unusually high levels of androgens, partially masculinizing the genitals of XX fetuses. Most girls and women with CAH have a female gender identity but tend to show more stereotypically masculine interests in childhood.
Delayed puberty
Puberty that has not begun by about age 14, usually caused by chronic illness, malnourishment, or a hormonal imbalance. Most cases can be treated.
Differences of Sex Development
A neutral medical term for what used to be called Disorders of Sex Development. The shift reflects criticism of language that implies intersex bodies are broken.
Disorders of Sex Development (DSDs)
A medical term for conditions in which sex development does not follow either of the two typical paths. The term has been criticized for implying intersex bodies are defective; many writers now prefer Differences of Sex Development.
Gender expression
How a person displays their gender to the world through clothing, hairstyle, voice, mannerisms, name, and pronouns.
Gender identity
A person's internal sense of being a man, a woman, both, or neither. It is psychological rather than biological and usually, but not always, matches the sex assigned at birth.
Gender schema theory
Sandra Bem's (1981) theory that children build a mental framework about what counts as masculine and feminine, then use that framework to interpret new experiences.
Gender similarities hypothesis
Janet Hyde's (2005) claim, based on decades of research, that men and women are far more alike than different on most psychological traits and abilities.
Genital swellings
Two outer folds of tissue in the early embryo that become the scrotum in males or the outer labia in females.
Genital tubercle
A small bump of tissue in the early embryo that becomes the glans of the penis in males or the glans of the clitoris in females.
Glans of the clitoris
The most sensitive, externally visible part of the clitoris. It develops from the same embryonic tissue (the genital tubercle) as the glans of the penis.
Glans of the penis
The rounded head of the penis at the tip of the shaft. It develops from the same embryonic tissue (the genital tubercle) as the glans of the clitoris.
Hermaphrodite
An older medical term for an intersex person, now considered inaccurate and offensive. The preferred terms today are intersex or Differences of Sex Development.
Homologous organs
Body parts that develop from the same embryonic tissue, even if they end up looking very different. The penis and clitoris are homologous, as are the scrotum and outer labia.
Hypothalamus
A small structure at the base of the brain that helps regulate hunger, thirst, body temperature, sleep, and sexual response. Some of its regions show consistent size differences between male and female brains.
Inguinal canal
A passageway in the lower abdominal wall through which the testes descend from the abdomen to the scrotum before birth.
Inner labia
Two folds of soft tissue surrounding the vaginal opening, just inside the outer labia. They develop from the same embryonic tissue (the urethral folds) as the shaft of the penis.
Intersex
A person born with reproductive or sexual anatomy that does not fit the typical male or female patterns. Estimates of prevalence range from about 1 in 1,000 births to roughly 1 percent, depending on which conditions are counted.
Klinefelter syndrome (XXY)
An intersex condition affecting about 1 in 1,000 male births, in which the person has one Y chromosome plus two or more X chromosomes. Common features include smaller testes, lower testosterone, less body hair, and taller-than-average height.
Menarche
A girl's first menstrual period, marking the beginning of fertility. In the United States, menarche occurs on average around age 12.5.
Müllerian ducts
A pair of ducts in the early embryo that develop into the female internal reproductive tract (fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, and upper vagina). In males, anti-Müllerian hormone causes them to disappear.
Neurosexism
A critical term for research that exaggerates biological differences between male and female brains, often using small or weak findings to support broad claims about behavior.
Outer labia
Two larger folds of skin and tissue surrounding the inner labia and vaginal opening. They develop from the same embryonic tissue (the genital swellings) as the scrotum.
Parental investment theory
An evolutionary theory (Trivers, 1972) proposing that the sex which invests more biological resources in offspring should be choosier about mates, while the sex which invests less should be more open to short-term mating.
Percent body fat hypothesis
Frisch and McArthur's (1974) proposal that menarche cannot begin until a girl's body has stored enough fat (roughly 17 to 22 percent) to support a possible pregnancy.
Precocious puberty
Puberty that begins unusually early, before age 8 in girls or age 9 in boys. Common contributors include childhood obesity and exposure to endocrine disruptors.
Prenatal sex differentiation
The developmental process by which a single fertilized egg becomes a body that is recognizably male, female, or somewhere in between.
Puberty
The developmental period during which a child's body transforms into an adult body capable of sexual reproduction. It typically begins between ages 9 and 11 in girls and between ages 10 and 12 in boys.
Scrotum
A loose sac of skin behind the penis that contains the two testes. It develops from the same embryonic tissue (the genital swellings) as the outer labia.
Sex
The biological category of male or female, assigned at birth based on observable physical features. It is determined by chromosomes, prenatal hormones, and anatomy, but does not always fit cleanly into the two standard categories.
Sexual double standard
The pattern by which men and women face different social judgments for the same sexual behavior. A man with many partners is often admired; a woman with the same number is often shamed.
Sexual identity
The broadest of the gender-related terms, referring to the whole package of how a person understands themselves as a sexual being. It includes gender identity, gender expression, sexual orientation, body image, and unique sexual interests.
Sexual orientation
Which gender or genders a person is romantically and physically attracted to. It is independent of gender identity and gender expression.
Sexual scripts
Unwritten cultural rules about how sexual interactions are supposed to unfold—who initiates, who responds, how desire is expressed, and what counts as "normal" sex.
Sexually dimorphic nucleus (SDN)
A region of the hypothalamus that tends to be larger in male brains than in female brains.
Shaft of the penis
The long, visible middle section of the penis between the root and the glans. It forms when the urethral folds fuse along the midline during prenatal development.
Social learning
A theory associated with Albert Bandura that most gender differences are learned through reward, punishment, and imitation rather than being built in from birth.
Spectrum
A range of possibilities with many valid points along it, often used as an alternative to the binary view of sex and gender. The dimmer-switch image is a common illustration.
SRY gene
A small region on the Y chromosome (short for "sex-determining region of the Y") that triggers the bipotential gonads to develop into testes. Without it, they develop into ovaries by default.
Stereotype threat
The anxiety a person feels when they fear confirming a negative stereotype about a group they belong to. The threat itself can interfere with performance, producing the very outcome the person feared.
Testosterone
The principal androgen, or "male" sex hormone. It drives the development of male reproductive structures before birth and produces the deepening voice, increased muscle mass, and body hair of puberty.
Triple X syndrome (XXX)
An intersex condition affecting about 1 in 2,000 female births, in which the person has three X chromosomes. Most women with Triple X have no obvious symptoms and never know they have the condition.
Turner syndrome (XO)
An intersex condition affecting about 1 in 2,000 to 2,500 female births, in which the person has only one X chromosome. Common features include short stature, a slightly webbed neck, low-set ears, and underdeveloped ovaries.
Urethral folds
Two folds of tissue on either side of the genital tubercle in the early embryo. They fuse to form the shaft of the penis in males or remain separate as the inner labia in females.
Wolffian ducts
A pair of ducts in the early embryo that, in the presence of testosterone, develop into the male internal reproductive structures (epididymis, vas deferens, and seminal vesicles). In females, they break down and disappear.
XYY syndrome
An intersex condition affecting about 1 in 1,500 male births, in which the person has one X chromosome and two Y chromosomes. Most are taller than average; some have mild learning differences.
