**Conception, Pregnancy, and Childbirth**

# Conception

## The Journey to the Egg: It's Not a Race

You've probably seen the cartoon version: millions of sperm lined up like runners, a starting gun fires, and the single fastest, strongest swimmer muscles through to "win" the egg. It's a great story. It's also mostly wrong (Eisenbach & Giojalas, 2006).

Start with the numbers. A typical ejaculate holds 200 to 300 million sperm. Of those, only a few hundred ever make it anywhere near the egg (Williams et al., 1993). The trip is brutal, and most sperm never get past the early obstacles. But here's the key correction: the survivors don't simply out-sprint everyone else. They're *guided*, and they're *filtered*.

Think of the female reproductive tract less like a racetrack and more like a long trail with a series of checkpoints and a helpful but picky trail guide. The tract screens out sperm that are damaged or poorly shaped, so quality really does matter. But reaching the egg isn't about raw speed. It's about being in good shape *and* following the directions the tract gives.

There's a twist most people don't expect: sperm aren't even ready to fertilize anything when they first arrive. They have to be switched on inside the female tract through a ripening step called capacitation. Picture hikers reaching the trailhead and only then unpacking and gearing up for the real climb (Eisenbach & Giojalas, 2006).

Once they're geared up, how do the sperm find their way? Researchers have found three guidance systems working together:

- **Swimming against the current (rheotaxis).** Fluid in the tract flows gently in one direction, and sperm instinctively turn and swim against it, like a fish nosing upstream. Heading into the current happens to point them the right way (Miki & Clapham, 2013).

- **Following the warmth (thermotaxis).** The spot where the egg waits is slightly warmer than where the sperm pause to gear up. Sperm can sense that tiny temperature difference and steer toward the heat (Bahat et al., 2003).

- **Following the scent (chemotaxis).** As they get close, sperm pick up chemical signals given off near the egg, almost like following a smell to its source, and home in (Eisenbach & Giojalas, 2006).

So the real story isn't a brawl won by the toughest swimmer. It's a guided journey, with the egg and the tract sending out signals that steer a small, healthy group of travelers toward the finish.

## Does the Body Pick the Winner? A Research Frontier

Here's a question scientists are still working out. Among the sperm that make it, is the "winner" basically random, or does the female body get a say in which one succeeds?

A handful of recent studies hint that it may not be random. In lab experiments, researchers mixed sperm from different men with fluid from different women's reproductive tracts. Some pairings worked noticeably better than others: the fluid seemed to help certain men's sperm and not others, and even to chemically attract some over others (Fitzpatrick et al., 2020; Jokiniemi et al., 2020). Studies in animals point in a similar direction (Løvlie et al., 2013). The idea even has a name, cryptic female choice. It is "cryptic" because it would be happening out of sight, down at the level of cells.

It's a genuinely intriguing possibility, so it's worth being clear about what we do and don't know. These are early findings. Most come from just a few research groups, and they were done in lab dishes, not inside the body. They'll need to be repeated by other scientists before anyone can say how much this matters for real pregnancies. For now, file it under "promising frontier," not "settled fact." Still, it's a useful hint that the egg and the tract may be far more active partners in fertilization than the old race story ever suggested.

## Meet the Sperm

We've followed sperm on their guided trip up the female tract. But we skipped two things along the way: what a sperm actually *is*, and how it got out of the body to begin with. Let's fill those in, and then watch the main event, where a sperm finally meets the egg.

A sperm cell is basically a tiny delivery vehicle, built for one job: carry a half-set of DNA to the egg. It has three parts:

- **The head** holds the cargo, the father's DNA, packed tightly inside. Capping the head is the acrosome, a little pouch of enzymes we'll need in a minute.

- **The midpiece** is the engine room, stuffed with mitochondria that burn fuel to power the trip.

- **The tail** is the propeller, whipping back and forth to push the sperm forward.

Cargo up front, engine in the middle, propeller in back: simple and purpose-built.

Sperm don't rush straight out the moment they're made. They finish maturing and wait in a coiled storage tube called the epididymis. During ejaculation, they travel up a tube (the vas deferens) and mix with fluids from a few glands. That mix is semen, and it's more than just a delivery liquid: it feeds the sperm with sugar for energy and shields them from the acidic environment of the vagina, which would otherwise kill them off fast. A typical ejaculate is only about a teaspoon of fluid, but it carries 200 to 300 million sperm (Silverthorn, 2013).

## Fertilization: One Sperm, One Egg

Now the meeting. Compared to a sperm, the egg is enormous, and it doesn't sit there unprotected. It's wrapped in a tough, jelly-like shell called the zona pellucida, with a cloud of helper cells around it.

This is where that acrosome cap pays off. When a sperm reaches the egg, the cap bursts open and releases its enzymes, which dissolve a tunnel through the jelly shell, like a key that chews its own keyhole. The sperm wriggles through and fuses with the egg.

And then the door slams shut. The instant one sperm gets in, the egg reacts within seconds: it sets off a dramatic burst of zinc, nicknamed the "zinc spark," that hardens the outer shell so no other sperm can bind or break through (Duncan et al., 2016; Que et al., 2017). This is the block to polyspermy, and it matters enormously. The math has to be exact: one sperm, one egg. If two sperm got in, the new cell would carry too many chromosomes and wouldn't survive.

Where does all of this happen? Not in the uterus, as many people assume, but out near the far end of the fallopian tube (Silverthorn, 2013). The egg barely makes it away from the ovary before a sperm reaches it.

The result is a single new cell carrying DNA from both partners, the first cell of a possible new person. From here it begins to divide and drift down toward the uterus. Whether it implants, and the long story of pregnancy that may follow, is where the rest of this chapter begins.

## Detecting a Pregnancy

For most people, the first hint that something has changed is a missed period. It is the classic sign for a reason, but it is not proof. Stress, illness, hard travel, big swings in weight or exercise, and plain old irregular cycles can all delay a period or skip one entirely. Early pregnancy can bring other clues too: tender or tingling breasts, more trips to the bathroom, a wave of fatigue, and the nausea known as morning sickness, which keeps its own schedule and can hit at any hour. None of these settles the question on its own. To know for sure, you need a test.

Every pregnancy test, from the cheap drugstore strip to the one a clinic runs, is hunting for the same single molecule: **human chorionic gonadotropin**, or **hCG**. The developing placenta starts pumping out hCG soon after the embryo implants in the wall of the uterus. Because only a pregnancy produces it, finding hCG in the body is a reliable yes. The hormone first appears about a week to ten days after fertilization, then climbs fast, roughly doubling every two to three days in the early weeks (Cleveland Clinic, 2026). That steep climb is the whole reason timing matters.

A **home pregnancy test** is a urine test you can buy over the counter and use in a few minutes. Used correctly on or after the day of a missed period, the major brands claim accuracy around 99 percent (Mayo Clinic, 2022). The catch is the phrase "on or after." Test too early, before hCG has built up, and you can get a **false negative**, where the test says no when the answer is really yes. This is the single most common testing mistake. A few "early" tests advertise results several days before a missed period, but their accuracy drops the earlier you go (Cleveland Clinic, 2026). Two simple habits help: use the first urine of the morning, when hCG is most concentrated, and if an early test reads negative but your period still does not arrive, test again in a few days (Mayo Clinic, 2022).

A **blood test**, run at a clinic, is the other option. It can pick up hCG earlier than a urine test, can measure the exact amount, and from that amount can help estimate how far along a pregnancy is (Cleveland Clinic, 2026). It costs more and means a trip to a provider, so it is used most when an early or precise answer matters medically.

Here is the part the chemistry leaves out. Those few minutes waiting for a result are among the more emotionally loaded a person can have. The very same plus sign can land as the best news of someone's life or as a quiet catastrophe, depending entirely on whether the pregnancy is wanted. One person sees a future they have been hoping for; another sees school, work, money, or a relationship thrown into question. We carry that whole range into the rest of the chapter, because pregnancy is never only a biological event. It always arrives inside a particular life.

**Fun Fact: The Original Pee Stick Was a Bag of Seeds.** Long before drugstore strips, people improvised. An ancient Egyptian method, written on papyrus more than 3,000 years ago, had a woman urinate on bags of wheat and barley seeds for several days. If the grain sprouted, she was said to be pregnant. It sounds like pure superstition, yet when a researcher finally put it to the test in 1963, the method flagged a fair share of real pregnancies (Ghalioungui et al., 1963). The likely reason is that pregnancy urine is loaded with hormones such as estrogen, which can nudge seeds to grow. The Egyptians were wrong that the grain could predict the baby's sex, but they had stumbled onto something true: a pregnant body sends chemical signals out through its urine. That is the same basic idea behind the test sitting in your bathroom cabinet today.

# Pregnancy and Prenatal Development

Pregnancy tends to get described as a single condition, the way we say someone "is pregnant" as though it were one steady state stretched across nine months. Living through it is closer to three distinct seasons, each with its own rapidly changing body inside and its own version of the person carrying it. Clinicians mark those seasons as the three **trimesters**, roughly three months apiece, and the shifts from one to the next are big enough that it pays to take them one at a time.

First, a word about the calendar, because the counting trips up almost everyone. A pregnancy is dated from the first day of the **last menstrual period (LMP)**, not from the day of conception. The reason is practical: most people can name the day their last period started, while very few know the exact day a sperm met an egg. The quirk this creates is that ovulation, and therefore conception, usually lands about two weeks into the count. So a person who is "four weeks pregnant" has been carrying an embryo for only about two weeks. A full-term pregnancy runs about 40 weeks on this calendar, which is where the familiar "nine months" comes from. We will use weeks from the LMP throughout, the same way a clinic would.

## The First Trimester (Weeks 1 to 13)

### From one cell to an embryo

The single cell formed at fertilization does not stay single for long. As it drifts down the fallopian tube toward the uterus, it divides again and again, and within a few days it has become a hollow ball of cells called a **blastocyst**. That ball has already split into a working crew and a future passenger: an outer layer that will build the **placenta**, and an inner cluster that will become the baby. Around a week after fertilization, the blastocyst burrows into the blood-rich lining of the uterus, a step called implantation. This is the moment the pregnancy plugs into the parent's body, and it is when the placenta starts releasing the hCG that a pregnancy test can find.

For the next several weeks the passenger is called an **embryo**, and it is laying down the rough draft of nearly everything: the beginnings of a heart, a brain and spinal cord, a digestive tract, and the buds that become arms and legs. By the end of the twelfth week it looks unmistakably human, with fingers, toes, eyelids, and a heartbeat a provider can detect, and from this point on it is called a **fetus** (Cunningham et al., 2010). Because so many major structures are being built in these first weeks, the first trimester is also the most vulnerable stretch of the whole pregnancy. A harmful exposure does the most damage when an organ is under construction, which is why the advice to avoid certain substances matters most early, a topic we take up in the next section.

### The support system: placenta, cord, and sac

Three structures keep the fetus alive, and all three are worth knowing because they explain a lot of what follows.

The **placenta** is the supply depot and the customs checkpoint rolled into one. It is the surface where the parent's blood and the fetus's blood run close enough to trade goods without ever mixing. Oxygen and nutrients cross from parent to fetus; carbon dioxide and waste cross back the other way. The placenta filters out many threats, so a parent can have a nasty cold while the fetus stays fine, but the filter is not airtight: some viruses and many drugs slip across, which is the reason a pregnant person has to be careful about what enters their body. The placenta is also a hormone factory, producing the estrogen and progesterone that keep the pregnancy stable, plus the hCG that announced the pregnancy in the first place.

The **umbilical cord** is the supply line that runs between the fetus and the placenta, carrying blood back and forth through about twenty inches of flexible tubing. The spot where it attaches becomes the belly button after birth.

The **amniotic sac** is the padded room. The fetus floats inside it in warm **amniotic fluid**, which holds a steady temperature and cushions every bump from the outside world. If the pregnant person trips and falls, the fluid absorbs the jolt and the fetus rides it out undisturbed. It is, in effect, the original waterbed.

### What the mother feels

Most of the early symptoms of pregnancy trace back to a single cause: a flood of hormones. hCG climbs steeply in the first weeks, and estrogen and progesterone rise alongside it, first from the corpus luteum left behind at ovulation and then, by about week 12, from the placenta taking over the job.

The most famous of these symptoms is **morning sickness**, the nausea and queasiness that affects roughly 70 percent of pregnant people (Einarson et al., 2013). The name is a small lie. The sickness can strike at any hour, and most people who get it feel it on and off throughout the day rather than only at dawn, often worse when they are tired or running on an empty stomach. For most it is miserable but harmless and fades by the second trimester. For a small share, around 1 percent, it becomes **hyperemesis gravidarum**, a severe form with relentless vomiting and weight loss that can require hospital treatment.

**Fun Fact: Feeling Awful Might Be a Good Sign.** Morning sickness seems pointless and cruel, but it may be doing a job. People who experience nausea and vomiting in early pregnancy are, on average, *less* likely to miscarry than those who sail through symptom-free (Flaxman & Sherman, 2000). One leading explanation is that the queasiness is protective by design: it peaks exactly when the embryo's organs are forming and most fragile, and it pushes the pregnant person away from strong-tasting, bitter, or spoiled foods, the very things most likely to carry toxins or bacteria. By that logic, a rough first trimester is the body playing it safe on the embryo's behalf.

Other early changes pile on: tender or tingling breasts, deep fatigue, frequent urination, food cravings, and sudden aversions to foods or smells that were fine a week earlier. Many of these overlap with ordinary premenstrual symptoms, which is exactly why the early signs alone cannot confirm a pregnancy and why a test does the real work.

### The couple's experience

For expectant parents, the first trimester is often a tangle of opposite feelings at once. There is excitement and a sense of accomplishment, and right beside it real anxiety: about the body changes underway, about the weight of becoming responsible for another person, and, for many, about whether the pregnancy will hold. The first trimester carries the highest risk of miscarriage, a loss we treat more fully later in the chapter, and that possibility quietly shadows the early weeks for a lot of couples.

There is also a built-in asymmetry that can strain a relationship before anyone expects it. For the pregnant person, the pregnancy is concrete from day one, because their body is the thing changing. For a partner, it can stay abstract for weeks, an idea more than an experience, until an ultrasound image or a first kick makes it land. That gap in felt reality is normal, but couples who do not see it coming sometimes read it as the partner not caring, when it is closer to the partner not yet feeling.

**Fun Fact: Some Partners Get Pregnancy Symptoms Too.** A surprising number of expectant fathers develop their own pregnancy-like symptoms, including nausea, food cravings, fatigue, and weight gain. The phenomenon is called **couvade**, or sympathetic pregnancy, and it shows up across many cultures (Kiselica & Scheckel, 1995). It is not pure imagination, either. Studies that tracked hormones in expectant fathers found real shifts, including a rise in prolactin and a dip in testosterone around the birth, the same general direction of change seen in the pregnant partner and a pattern linked to nurturing behavior (Storey et al., 2000). In some cultures the experience is formalized into a couvade ritual, in which the father takes to bed and acts out the pains of labor while his partner delivers.

## The Second Trimester (Weeks 14 to 27)

If the first trimester is the rough patch, the second is often the reward. For most people the nausea lifts, energy comes back, and the pregnancy starts to feel less like an illness and more like an event.

### The developing fetus

The fetus grows fast now and starts doing recognizably human things. Sometime around weeks 16 to 20, the pregnant person feels the first flutters of movement, a milestone called **quickening**. First-time parents often notice it later than people who have been pregnant before, simply because they are not yet sure what they are feeling. By the middle of the trimester the fetus can hear, responds to light, and goes through cycles of rest and activity.

This trimester is also where the concept of **viability** enters the picture, meaning the point at which a fetus could survive outside the womb. Early in the second trimester it cannot: the lungs are far too immature to move oxygen on their own. The edge of viability currently sits around 22 to 24 weeks, and survival climbs steeply with each added week. In a large 2024 study of U.S. neonatal intensive care units, survival rose from about 25 percent for infants born at 22 weeks to about 82 percent for those born at 25 weeks (Edwards et al., 2024). Below roughly 22 weeks, survival remains extremely unlikely even with the best available care. These are sobering numbers, but they also mark how far newborn medicine has come, since the same births were almost uniformly fatal a few decades ago.

### What changes for the mother

With hCG leveling off, the early-pregnancy nausea fades for most people by about week 13, and a stretch of relative comfort and energy follows. The increase in blood volume that supports the pregnancy can give the skin the warm, flushed look people call the "pregnancy glow."

The visible changes arrive in this trimester. The uterus, normally about the size of a pear, expands to dozens of times its prior size and pushes the surrounding organs upward, which is why many people find they can no longer eat a large meal and have to switch to smaller, more frequent ones. The growing uterus also presses on the bladder, so the bathroom trips continue. The breasts enlarge and the areolae darken as the body prepares to feed a newborn, and some people start to produce tiny amounts of **colostrum**, the rich early milk. Stretch marks may appear across the belly and breasts, and the extra blood volume can cause **edema**, a mild swelling of the hands and feet.

### The couple's experience

Emotionally, the second trimester tends to be the calm in the middle. The anxieties of the first trimester have eased, the worries about labor are not yet pressing, and the pregnancy feels more secure. It is often the point where a partner finally connects to the pregnancy in a felt way. Watching an ultrasound and, especially, placing a hand on the belly to feel the baby kick turns the abstract into something real. That shared, physical experience tends to draw couples closer, and a supportive partner during this stretch is one of the better predictors of lower anxiety as the pregnancy goes on.

## The Third Trimester (Weeks 28 to 40)

The home stretch is the most physical part of the journey. The fetus is finishing its preparations for life outside, and the pregnant person is carrying a great deal more than they were three months earlier.

### The developing fetus

In these final months the brain and nervous system mature rapidly, and the lungs do their last and most important fine-tuning, which is why even an extra week or two in the womb can meaningfully improve a newborn's odds. The fetus is also packing on the fat that will help it regulate temperature after birth.

Most fetuses settle head-down in preparation for delivery, a position called **cephalic presentation**. A small share do not: about 3 to 4 percent are still bottom-first or feet-first at term, known as **breech** presentation (ACOG, 2020). Breech is far more common earlier in pregnancy and becomes rarer as the due date approaches, since the great majority of fetuses turn on their own in the final weeks. When a fetus stays breech, a provider may try to coax it head-down by pressing on the abdomen, a maneuver called **external cephalic version**, and if it remains breech, delivery is often by cesarean section, a topic for the next section.

A quick word on timing. Clinicians now reserve the term "full term" for births between 39 and 40 weeks, with 37 to 38 weeks labeled "early term," because research showed that even that last week or two improves outcomes. The takeaway built into those labels is that scheduled deliveries should generally wait until at least 39 weeks unless there is a medical reason not to.

### What changes for the mother

This is the uncomfortable trimester. The enlarged uterus crowds the lungs, stomach, and bladder, bringing shortness of breath, heartburn, back pain, hemorrhoids, and yet more frequent urination, along with more pronounced swelling in the hands, feet, and ankles. Recommended weight gain for a person who started at a healthy weight is about 25 to 35 pounds across the whole pregnancy, most of it in the second and third trimesters (Institute of Medicine, 2009).

A few late milestones stand out. Around weeks 34 to 36, the fetus often drops lower into the pelvis, a shift called lightening that eases the pressure on the lungs while adding to it on the bladder. Many people also begin to feel **Braxton-Hicks contractions**, irregular, usually painless tightenings that feel like a belt cinching across the lower belly for a few seconds. They are the uterus rehearsing for labor, and they grow more frequent in the final weeks. Between the physical bulk, the disrupted sleep, and the sheer wait, most people reach a point where they feel thoroughly ready to be done.

### The couple's experience

Anticipation runs high in the last weeks, mixed with a frank eagerness to have the physical pregnancy behind them. It is common for fresh anxieties to surface too: about the pain of labor, about the baby's health, and about whether one is ready to be a parent at all. Many couples channel that energy into preparation, the nesting impulse to ready a home, take a birth class, and handle the logistics of a new arrival.

Partners tend to step up their involvement here. First-time partners often worry about how to be useful during the birth itself, while those who have been through it before worry more about the pain their partner will face (Poh et al., 2014). The practical work of getting ready, the appointments, the classes, the rearranging of a household, is itself a kind of bonding, and it builds the foundation the new family will stand on.

## Sexuality During Pregnancy

Because this is a book about human sexuality, the question couples most often ask deserves a direct answer: is it safe to have sex while pregnant? For a normal, healthy pregnancy, the answer is yes, right up to the start of labor (Jones et al., 2011). The fetus is sealed off and well protected inside the amniotic sac, and a thick plug of mucus seals the cervix against germs from the outside. Despite a widespread fear, there is no evidence that intercourse or orgasm brings on labor early in a low-risk pregnancy (Sayle et al., 2001; Schaffir, 2006). The exceptions are specific: if there is a heightened risk of miscarriage or preterm labor, unexplained bleeding, or a problem with the placenta, a provider may advise holding off, since the mild uterine contractions that follow orgasm are best avoided in those situations. The guidance is tailored to the case, not a blanket rule.

Desire itself tends to move in a rough arc across the pregnancy. Interest often dips in the first trimester, when nausea and exhaustion are at their height, rebounds in the second trimester, and tapers again in the third as size and discomfort take over (Byrd et al., 1998; Hasani et al., 2019). The second-trimester rebound is more than just feeling better: the extra blood flow to the pelvis that comes with pregnancy can heighten sensitivity and, for some people, makes arousal and orgasm easier than before. None of this is universal, and the variation from person to person is wide.

Comfort becomes the practical guide, especially later on. As the belly grows, some positions stop working, and couples tend to find that side-lying arrangements are easier than anything with weight on the abdomen. It also helps to remember that intercourse is only one item on a long menu; manual and oral stimulation are perfectly good alternatives when intercourse is awkward. Discomfort is worth taking seriously rather than enduring in silence. About one in five people experience pain during intercourse at some point in pregnancy, and few mention it to a provider, even though it is a reasonable thing to raise (Rossi et al., 2019; Glowacka et al., 2014).

Partners' feelings shape the picture too. Some find the pregnant body especially appealing, while others feel hesitant, sometimes out of a misplaced worry about harming the baby, which they will not. Many couples also describe a subtle psychological shift, a sense that a third person is now present, heightened by the fact that fetal movement often picks up after sex and orgasm. Finally, a few signs are reasons to pause and call a provider rather than push on: pain, bleeding, leaking fluid, or a discharge that could be the mucus plug loosening, since any of these can signal a problem or the onset of labor.
