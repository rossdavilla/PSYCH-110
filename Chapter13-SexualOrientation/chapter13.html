Sexual Orientation & Gender Identity

Attraction, identity, and the many ways people come to understand who they love.

📝 Study Guide🔑 Key Terms

Think about the last time you felt a spark of attraction toward someone. You probably did not choose to feel it, and you probably could not have talked yourself out of it. That pull — toward men, toward women, or toward more than one gender — sits at the heart of what we call sexual orientation. It shapes who we date, who we fall in love with, the communities we join, and, for many people, how the wider world treats us.

Sexual orientation is about who you are attracted to. It is a different thing from gender identity, which is your own inner sense of your gender. However, given that the two concepts are often bundled together under the same umbrella (LGBTQ+) and in the same struggles for rights, this chapter will also include discussion of gender identity and related topics.

13.1 What Are Sexual Orientation & Gender Identity?

To whom are you attracted? Men? Women? More than one gender? People who do not fit neatly into either category? We tend to live in an either/or world. Sexual orientation is no exception to this habit of dividing everything into neat, non-overlapping categories. Many people assume you are simply gay or straight, with nothing in between. The reality is far more complex, and people rarely fit into tidy boxes.

It is also separate from a person's body. A person of any biological sex, and any gender, is capable of being attracted to people of any gender.

This is where a common mix-up happens, so let's clearly lay this out. Sexual orientation is who you are drawn to sexually and/or romantically. Being transgender tells you about a person's gender, but not about who they love.

Referring to someone exclusively or predominantly as a trans woman or trans man may feel "othering," as their goal is often simply to live as a woman or man like any other. A countermovement is to refer to non-trans women and men as cisgendered. This chapter will use the inclusive term LGBTQ+ to describe these various groups, with the acronym standing for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer, plus. The "plus" is doing a lot of heavy lifting, given all of the other orientation identities captured in this umbrella term. A more accurate acronym might be LGBTQ+QIPP2SAA, with the additions of questioning, intersex, pansexual, polyamorous, two-spirit, asexual, and ally. There are yet other terms that are generally recognized as non-straight identities, such as Flexual (flexible/fluid sexuality), gender-nonconforming, gender-fluid, androgynous, and "genderfuck," the latter being a term used to subvert and mock traditional gender roles.

Identity Is Not the Same as Behavior

The label a person identifies with does not necessarily describe the kind of sexual behavior they engage with. To put it plainly, lots of people who identify as straight have sex with people of the same gender. These experiences may be occasional or even routine. This does not mean anyone is confused or lying. It means that identity, attraction, and behavior are three separate things that do not always line up.

Psychologist Fritz Klein captured this in the 1970s with a tool called the Klein Sexual Orientation GridiKlein Sexual Orientation GridFritz Klein's tool that measures orientation across several ingredients — attraction, behavior, fantasy, emotional and social preference, self-label, and change over time.View in glossary → (Klein, 1978)ReferenceKlein, F. (1978). The bisexual option. Arbor House.. Klein argued that orientation is not one thing but a bundle of at least seven ingredients: who you are sexually attracted to, who you have sex with, who you fantasize about, who you feel emotionally close to, who you socialize with, how you label yourself, and how all of this might shift over time. A single person can land in different places on these dimensions — perhaps being deeply in love with a partner of one gender while fantasizing about another. Klein's grid makes room for the messiness of real life sexuality.

Measuring Something That Resists Measurement

Klein was building on a famous earlier effort. In 1948, sex researcher Alfred Kinsey shocked the public by reporting that same-sex behavior was far more common than anyone had once believed (Kinsey et al., 1948)ReferenceKinsey, A. C., Pomeroy, W. B., & Martin, C. E. (1948). Sexual behavior in the human male. W. B. Saunders.. Instead of sorting people into "heterosexual" and "homosexual," Kinsey had his participants place themselves on a scale. The Kinsey ScaleiKinsey ScaleA 0-to-6 scale, introduced by Alfred Kinsey, placing people along a continuum from exclusively heterosexual to exclusively homosexual.View in glossary → runs from 0 (completely heterosexual) to 6 (completely homosexual), with the numbers in between capturing various shades of bisexuality. Kinsey insisted that people were not permanently glued to one number but could move around on the scale across their lives and with different partners.

Kinsey's scale was a breakthrough, but it had a flaw. By putting heterosexuality and homosexuality at opposite ends of a single line, it implied that being more attracted to one gender automatically meant being less attracted to the other. Later researchers fixed this by using two separate scales — one measuring attraction to men, another measuring attraction to women. That way, a person could be high on both, low on both, or anywhere in between. Two-dimensional models, and richer tools like Klein's grid, give us a far more accurate picture than a single either/or question ever could.

Orientation Across Cultures and Time

It is tempting to assume that the categories we use today are timeless. They are not. The words heterosexual and homosexual did not even exist until the late 1800s (Katz, 2007)ReferenceKatz, J. N. (2007). The invention of heterosexuality. University of Chicago Press.. People have always had same-sex relationships, but the idea that such attraction defines a whole type of person is a fairly recent idea.

Other cultures have organized sexuality in strikingly different ways. Among many Indigenous North American peoples, Two-SpiritiTwo-SpiritA term used by some Indigenous North American peoples for individuals who blend masculine and feminine roles, historically often honored as spiritual leaders.View in glossary → individuals — who blend masculine and feminine roles — have often been honored as spiritual leaders (Robinson, 2020)ReferenceRobinson, M. In some societies, same-sex relationships have been an expected stage of life that people pass through before marriage. The lesson is not that any one culture has it right, but that the tidy gay/straight split many of us grew up with is just one view of a particular place and time.

13.2 How Common Is It? Prevalence and Fluidity

Before reading on, take a guess: out of every 100 Americans, how many do you think identify as lesbian, gay, bisexual, or transgender?

Surveys find that the average American estimates that roughly one in four people — about 24% — is gay or lesbian (Gallup, 2019)ReferenceGallup. (2019). Americans still greatly overestimate U.S. gay population. https://news.gallup.com/poll/259571. As you'll soon read, the actual number is quite a bit lower.

LGBTQ+ Demographics

The most recent national polling finds that about 9% of U.S. adults identify as lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, or something other than straight (Gallup, 2026a)ReferenceGallup. (2026a). LGBTQ+ identification in the U.S. https://news.gallup.com/poll/702206. That figure has more than doubled since 2012, when it was 3.5%. Within that 9%, the largest group by far is bisexual people, who make up more than half of all LGBTQ+ adults. Gay and lesbian people each account for roughly 1–2% of adults, and transgender people also make up a little over 1% (Gallup, 2026a)ReferenceGallup. (2026a). LGBTQ+ identification in the U.S. https://news.gallup.com/poll/702206.

The most striking pattern is generational. Among adults under 30 — Generation Z — about 23% identify as LGBTQ+, compared with roughly 10% of adults in their thirties and forties and only 2 to 3% of adults over 50 (Gallup, 2026a)ReferenceGallup. (2026a). LGBTQ+ identification in the U.S. https://news.gallup.com/poll/702206. Why the steep jump? Almost certainly it is not that today's young people are biologically different from their grandparents. Given that stigma against LGBTQ+ people has decreased greatly over the past few decades, young people may feel safer publicly claiming an identity that earlier generations would have kept hidden. There is also a large gender gap among the young: close to a third of Gen Z women identify as LGBTQ+, compared with about one in eight Gen Z men, and most of that difference comes from women identifying as bisexual (Gallup, 2026a)ReferenceGallup. (2026a). LGBTQ+ identification in the U.S. https://news.gallup.com/poll/702206.

As discussed before, sexual identity and sexual behavior are two different things. About 17% of women and 6% of men report having had sex with someone of the same gender at some point in their lives. Of the women who report same-sex experiences, most say they are attracted almost exclusively to men, and many identify as straight (Copen et al., 2016)ReferenceCopen, C. E., Chandra, A., & Febo-Vazquez, I. (2016). Sexual behavior, sexual attraction, and sexual orientation among adults aged 18–44 in the United States: Data from the 2011–2013 National Survey of Family Growth. National Health Statistics Reports, (88), 1–14.. This isn't as true for men, with only a minority of men who have had sex with other men self-labeling as straight. This may mean that women are more likely to "experiment" with same-gender sex, even if they're primarily attracted to members of the opposite sex. It could also mean that women are simply more willing to report having same-gender sex than men are. It's impossible to say, given that all of this data is self-report.

Who Are LGBTQ+ People?

Ask people to picture a "typical" gay person and many imagine a white, wealthy, college-educated man in a big coastal city. The data tell a different story. Women make up the majority of LGBTQ+ adults, a large share being people of color (Gallup, 2026a)ReferenceGallup. (2026a). LGBTQ+ identification in the U.S. https://news.gallup.com/poll/702206. LGBTQ+ people live in every region of the country, not just the coasts. Rates are broadly similar across racial and ethnic groups. LGBTQ+ people are also, on average, no wealthier than anyone else — and some groups within the community face higher rates of poverty, particularly people who are transgender. The glossy stereotype is mostly a media creation, not a description of real LGBTQ+ people.

Sexual & Gender Fluidity

For many people, orientation feels fixed from early on. For others, it shifts. In one long-running study, about two-thirds of the women changed how they labeled themselves over roughly a decade, and about a third changed more than once (Diamond, 2008)ReferenceDiamond, L. M. (2008). Sexual fluidity: Understanding women's love and desire. Harvard University Press.. Importantly, more women adopted the bisexual label than dropped it, which suggests that bisexuality is a stable destination for many, not just a "pit stop" on the way to being gay or straight.

Sexual fluidity is not equal across genders. On average, women's sexuality tends to be more fluid than men's, and men tend to show more consistent, narrowly focused patterns of arousal (Diamond, 2014)ReferenceDiamond, L. M. (2014). Gender and same-sex sexuality. In D. L. Tolman & L. M. Diamond (Eds.), APA handbook of sexuality and psychology (Vol. 1, pp. 629–652). American Psychological Association.. This is a difference in averages, not a rule about any individual — plenty of men experience shifts, and plenty of women feel fixed. It is also part of why, across nearly every survey, women are roughly twice as likely as men to identify as bisexual.

13.3 What Shapes Sexual Orientation?

Researchers who study sexual orientation are often asked, "What makes someone gay?" Pause a moment and ask yourself why this might not be the right question. We rarely demand to know what "causes" heterosexuality. The very framing smuggles in the assumption that being straight is the natural default and everything else needs explaining. This assumption is termed heterosexismiHeterosexismThe built-in assumption that everyone is, or should be, heterosexual; usually unintentional but marginalizing. Also called heteronormativity.View in glossary →. This is why scientists have tried to tackle the issue more broadly, asking what factors determine a person's sexual orientation, whether straight or LGBTQ+.

Nature vs. Nurture

For much of the twentieth century, psychologists leaned on nurture-based explanations. Sigmund Freud, for instance, speculated that male homosexuality grew out of a particular family pattern, such as an overbearing mother and a distant father. Theories like this have not survived decades of careful study. Consider three findings that pure-upbringing accounts cannot explain.

  • Sexual orientation runs in biological families, with some families having more LGBTQ+ members than you would expect by chance (Bailey & Pillard, 1991; Bailey et al., 1993)ReferencesBailey, J. M., & Pillard, R. C. (1991). A genetic study of male sexual orientation. Archives of General Psychiatry, 48(12), 1089–1096. https://doi.org/10.1001/archpsyc.1991.01810360053008

    Bailey, J. M., Pillard, R. C., Neale, M. C., & Agyei, Y. (1993). Heritable factors influence sexual orientation in women. Archives of General Psychiatry, 50(3), 217–223.    . And, it's not that being raised in a "gay friendly" family causes this trend. When someone is adopted into these family lines, they're not more likely to be LGBTQ+ than average. When someone is adopted out of these family lines and into families with low rates of LGBTQ+ orientations, that adopted person is more likely to be LGBTQ+ than average (for a review, see Bragazzi et al., 2026ReferenceBragazzi, N., Farah, R., & Khamisy-Farah, R. (2026). Unraveling the genetics of sexual orientation: A historical and contemporary perspective. The Israel Medical Association Journal, 28, 126–132.). This is exactly what you would find with other genetic traits, such as eye color. Clearly, there is something in our genetics related to sexual orientation. We now have even clearer evidence from large-scale genomic studies, which find that sexual orientation is significantly tied to specific genes (Ganna et al., 2019)ReferenceGanna, A., Verweij, K. J.   • Children who are raised by same-gender parents are no more likely to end up LGBTQ+ themselves than someone in the general population. K. (2016). Same-sex and different-sex parent households and child health outcomes. Journal of Developmental & Behavioral Pediatrics, 37(3), 179–187. https://doi.org/10.1097/DBP.0000000000000288.
  • Same-sex behavior turns out to be incredibly common throughout the animal kingdom. There are thousands of known species where a small, but significant proportion of animals (often 2–10%) seem to prefer same-gender sex over opposite-gender sex (Bailey & Zuk, 2009; Gómez et al., 2023)ReferencesBailey, N. Same-gender sex is commonly found among lions, flamingos, and even fruit flies. If same-gender attraction was the product of nurture factors, such as upbringing or culture, we wouldn't expect to find it in non-human animals.

Genes and Heritability

If orientation is partly inherited, how much? The clearest evidence comes from twin studies and large genetic analyses. Identical twins, who share essentially all of their DNA, are more likely to share an orientation than fraternal twins, who do not. The largest study of its kind analyzed the DNA of nearly half a million people and estimated that genetics account for roughly a third — about 32% — of the differences between people in same-sex behavior (Ganna et al., 2019)ReferenceGanna, A., Verweij, K. J. H., Nivard, M. G., Maier, R., Wedow, R., Busch, A. S., Abdellaoui, A., Guo, S., Sathirapongsasuti, J. F., 23andMe Research Team, … Zietsch, B. P. (2019). Large-scale GWAS reveals insights into the genetic architecture of same-sex sexual behavior. Science, 365(6456), eaat7693. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aat7693.

Two points from that study matter enormously. First, there is no single "gay gene." Instead, thousands of genes each make a tiny contribution, which means no genetic test could ever predict a person's orientation. Second, genes are only part of the story, leaving about two-thirds of variance in sexual orientation unexplained. As you'll soon read, the remaining influence likely comes from other biological factors outside of our genome.

Some research also points to epigeneticsiEpigeneticsChemical tags that switch genes on or off without changing the DNA itself; epigenetic marks are one proposed influence on the development of sexual orientation.View in glossary →, chemical tags that switch genes on or off without changing the DNA itself, as another piece of the puzzle. For example, a study by Rice and colleagues (2012)ReferenceRice, W. R., Friberg, U., & Gavrilets, S. (2012). Homosexuality as a consequence of epigenetically canalized sexual development. The Quarterly Review of Biology, 87(4), 343–368. https://doi.org/10.1086/668167 found that sexually antagonistic epi-marks were less prevalent in lesbians than in straight women. These epi-marks typically buffer a female fetus from being "masculinized" by natural surges in a pregnant woman's testosterone levels. It is believed that hormones play a major role in the development of a person's sexual orientation, especially during fetal gestation. Another study has found that epigenetic markers may predict a person's sexual orientation with up to 70% accuracy, indicating this is a valuable area for future research (Balter, 2015)ReferenceBalter, M. (2015). Can epigenetics explain homosexuality puzzle? Science, 350(6257), 148. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.350.6257.148.

Use the steps below to test your own 2D:4D ratio:

  1. Step 1: Check your right hand. The correlation is statistically strongest on the right hand due to how the right hemisphere of the body interacts with early hormones.
  2. Step 2: Look at the base creases. Find the very bottom crease where your index finger (2D) and ring finger (4D) connect to your palm. If there is a double crease, use the line closest to your wrist.
  3. Step 3: Measure the length. Use a ruler to measure from that exact bottom crease straight up to the very tip of the fleshy pad of the finger (do not include your fingernail).
  4. Step 4: Divide the numbers. Divide the length of your index finger by the length of your ring finger (2D / 4D).

The Verdict: If your result is below 0.96, you have a "low" or masculine ratio, meaning your ring finger is noticeably longer than your index finger. If your result is around 1.00 or higher, you have a "high" or feminine ratio, meaning your index finger is equal to or longer than your ring finger.

The Fraternal Birth Order Effect

One of the most reliably replicated findings in this field is also one of the strangest. On average, gay men have more older brothers than straight men do; and the effect comes specifically from older brothers, not older sisters. All of these different lines of evidence point toward sexual orientation being a product of a complex interaction between different biological inputs — from genes and epigenetics to prenatal hormones and birth-order effects. What's clear are two things: 1) A person's sexual orientation is likely not rooted in their upbringing, culture or other nurture factors, and 2) Sexual orientation is primarily determined by the time someone is born. This isn't to say that a person's sexual identity cannot be fluid or change over the course of their lifetime — research by Diamond and others shows this can clearly happen. But, a person's tendency toward a particular identity or fluidity between identities is something that is largely determined in the womb.

13.4 Living as a Sexual Minority

Knowing what orientation is and where it comes from is only part of the picture. The rest is lived experience: figuring out who you are, deciding when and whether to tell others, finding community, and building relationships and families.

Coming Out

For most people, being straight requires no announcement; the world simply assumes it. LGBTQ+ people do not get that option. The process of coming outiComing outThe lifelong, repeated process of publicly acknowledging one's LGBTQ+ identity, which happens again and again because people are usually assumed to be straight.View in glossary → — publicly acknowledging one's identity — is not a single dramatic moment, but a lifelong, repeated process. Because people are assumed to be straight until they say otherwise, an LGBTQ+ person "comes out" again and again — to family, to new friends, to coworkers, to a new doctor.

Researchers have mapped this journey into stages. One of the more influential theories is the Cass Identity Model (1979)ReferenceCass, V. C. (1979). Homosexual identity formation: A theoretical model. Journal of Homosexuality, 4(3), 219–235. https://doi.org/10.1300/J082v04n03_01, which includes 6 stages:

Stage 1: Identity Confusion

The person experiences their first conscious thoughts, feelings or attractions toward members of the same sex. This is often confusing and can be distressing, as it violates the "default straight" mode we all grow up with. It's common for people in this stage to deny any same-sex attractions they have, even to themselves.

Stage 2: Identity Comparison

The person begins to accept the possibility that they may not be straight, but they aren't ready to acknowledge it more broadly. They may think, "I might be gay, but not like them," as they realize that adopting an LGBTQ+ identity means giving up societal privileges and expectations for the typical heterosexual life (e.g., traditional marriage, family approval).

Stage 3: Identity Tolerance

The person comes to be more accepting of a burgeoning queer identity, but may feel isolated and lonely. This will optimally drive them to seek out other people in the LGBTQ+ community. This can mean visiting queer spaces, but more often now means using dating apps or other online apps to meet queer people.

Stage 4: Identity Acceptance

"I am gay and that's okay." This famous saying epitomizes this stage, as the person moves from merely tolerating their new identity to fully embracing it. The person starts to develop a positive narrative about their queer identity, often sharing this identity with family and close friends.

Stage 5: Identity Pride

One of the more controversial stages of this model, stage 5 holds that the person will fully immerse themselves in LGBTQ+ culture and may develop strong "us vs them" thinking about straight people. They may view heterosexuals as "the enemy" or "the oppressors" and become a "loud and proud" advocate for fellow queer people in their community. This stage is controversial, because only a small minority of LGBTQ+ people actively participate in pride rallies or directly confront homophobic people in their community. This stage also risks reinforcing the stereotype of the politically "radical" and "leftist" lesbian or gay man.

Stage 6: Identity Synthesis

"Being gay is an important part of me, but not the only part of me." At this stage, the anger and division of the preceding stage have softened. The person may come to appreciate all of the supportive straight people in their lives. This is often heralded as the "goal" of coming out, achieving an effortless, authentic way of living.

The Cass model has been adapted into many other coming out models over the years, but they all have similar elements — going from denial to acceptance to pride (Coleman, 1982)ReferenceColeman, E. (1982). Developmental stages of the coming out process. Journal of Homosexuality, 7(2–3), 31–43. https://doi.org/10.1300/J082v07n02_06. These models can offer a useful roadmap for the coming out process, but they don't describe everyone's journeys. Most queer people don't experience all of the stages, and there are many examples of people traveling backward or stalling out on this journey. For transgender people, coming out often includes socially or medically transitioning — a related but separate journey from disclosing a sexual orientation.

Disclosure is far from universal. Many LGBTQ+ people are out to some people and not others, and a large share never come out to their parents or other family members. Bisexual people, in particular, are considerably less likely than gay men or lesbians to be out to the important people in their lives, partly because their identity is so often doubted or dismissed (Pew Research Center, 2013)ReferencePew Research Center. 13.5 Prejudice, Progress, and Support

Throughout history, people have treated LGBTQ+ people with a wide range of responses, from open hostility to meaningful acceptance. The pendulum has swung many times, with prejudice and discrimination easing and then returning time and time again. Rather than delve into the full timeline of gay history, we'll focus on what psychologically drives prejudice and what mechanisms have been reliably shown to reduce stigma.

The Many Faces of Prejudice

Prejudice against LGBTQ+ people ranges from quiet assumptions to open hatred. At the subtle end is heterosexism (also called heteronormativityiHeteronormativityThe built-in assumption that everyone is, or should be, heterosexual; another term for heterosexism.View in glossary →), the built-in assumption that everyone is straight and that being straight is the norm. This often shows up in unintended ways, such as asking a young man whether he has a girlfriend, or assuming a wedding ring means a person has an opposite-sex spouse. While often unintentional, it quietly signals that non-straight lives are unexpected and undervalued. Further along the spectrum is homophobiaiHomophobiaA pattern of fear, discomfort, or hostility toward gay people.View in glossary →, a term coined in the early 1970s for a pattern of fear, discomfort, or hostility toward queer people (Weinberg, 1972)ReferenceWeinberg, G. (1972). Society and the healthy homosexual. St. Martin's Press.. This can spill over into outright discrimination and even violence. Scientists often use the term sexual prejudiceiSexual prejudiceA broad term used by scientists for any form of negative attitude or prejudice directed at people because of their sexual orientation.View in glossary → to describe any form of prejudice toward sexual minorities (Herek, 2000)ReferenceHerek, G. M. (2000). The psychology of sexual prejudice. Current Directions in Psychological Science, 9(1), 19–22. https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8721.00051.

Where does sexual prejudice come from? Certainly, anti-queer messaging from family, peers, and institutions is one obvious source. Prejudice is fed by stereotypes, which often have little-to-no basis in reality. Gay men are stereotyped as flamboyant and hypersexual, lesbians as masculine and man-hating, and bisexual people as promiscuous or untrustworthy (Mize & Manago, 2018; Vaughn et al., 2017)ReferencesMize, T., & Manago, B. (2018). The stereotype content of sexual orientation. Social Currents, 5, 458–478. https://doi.org/10.1177/2329496518761999

Vaughn, A. A., Teeters, S. A., Sadler, M. S., & Cronan, S. B. (2017). Stereotypes, emotions, and behaviors toward lesbians, gay men, bisexual women, and bisexual men. Journal of Homosexuality, 64(13), 1890–1911. https://doi.org/10.1080/00918369.2016.1273718. When researchers measure things like relationship stability, number of partners, and relationship satisfaction, gay and straight people simply do not differ in the ways the stereotypes predict (APA, 2008; Chonody et al., 2020)ReferencesAmerican Psychological Association. (2008, January 23). Equal level of commitment and relationship satisfaction found among gay and heterosexual couples. ScienceDaily.

Chonody, J. M., Killian, M., Gabb, J., & Dunk-West, P. (2020). Relationship quality and sexuality: A latent profile analysis of long-term heterosexual and LGB long-term partnerships. Journal of Evidence-Based Social Work, 17(2), 203–225. https://doi.org/10.1080/26408066.2020.1723769. One especially damaging myth — that gay men are a danger to children — deserves direct correction: the research is clear that the vast majority of people who abuse children are heterosexual, and there is no evidence that gay men pose any greater risk to kids (Jenny et al., 1994; Seto, 2012)ReferencesJenny, C., Roesler, T. A., & Poyer, K. L. (1994). Are children at risk for sexual abuse by homosexuals? Pediatrics, 94(1), 41–44.

While many people may display sexual prejudice simply because they "absorbed" it from their surrounding culture, homophobia may play a defensive role for some. In one classic study, men who scored high on homophobia showed, on average, more physical arousal to same-sex imagery than men who scored low (Adams et al., 1996)ReferenceAdams, H. E., Wright, L. W., & Lohr, B. A. (1996). Is homophobia associated with homosexual arousal? Journal of Abnormal Psychology, 105(3), 440–445. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-843X.105.3.440. A more recent study found similar results, where men who espoused more homophobic beliefs were also more likely to subconsciously identify words related to themselves (e.g., "Me") with words related to being homosexual (e.g., "Gay"; Weinstein et al., 2012ReferenceWeinstein, N., Ryan, W. S., DeHaan, C. R., Przybylski, A. K., Legate, N., & Ryan, R. M. (2012). It's worth noting, though, that it's unlikely this drives sexual prejudice among most people.

Prejudice can also be internalized. When LGBTQ+ people absorb society's negative messages and turn them inward, the result can be shame, anxiety, and depression. Fun Fact: Hollywood's Sissies and Villains. For decades, American films almost never showed openly gay characters — not because none were written, but because they were forbidden. From 1934 to 1968, the industry's Hays Code effectively banned any positive depiction of homosexuality on screen (Russo, 1987)ReferenceRusso, V. (1987). Gay men were portrayed as fussy, effeminate "sissies," or as vain, menacing villains. While these characters were never outwardly called gay, generations absorbed these caricatures as if they typified the queer community. These coded depictions of LGBTQ+ sissies and villains can be found even in modern movies, such as the portrayals of Scar, Captain Hook, and Jafar in popular Disney movies.

The Toll on Mental Health

LGBTQ+ people, and LGBTQ+ teenagers in particular, experience higher rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidal thoughts than their straight and cisgender peers. It is essential to understand why. The explanation is not that being LGBTQ+ is itself a disorder — every major medical and psychological association rejects that idea. The accepted framework is the minority stress modeliMinority stress modelThe model holding that the higher rates of mental-health problems among LGBTQ+ people are caused by chronic stigma, rejection, and discrimination rather than by their identity.View in glossary →, which holds that it is the chronic burden of stigma, rejection, and discrimination that drives these mental-health gaps (Meyer, 2003)ReferenceMeyer, I. H. (2003). Prejudice, social stress, and mental health in lesbian, gay, and bisexual populations: Conceptual issues and research evidence. Psychological Bulletin, 129(5), 674–697. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.129.5.674. Unsurprisingly, perpetually being the target of prejudice and discrimination makes people unwell.

Schools are a frequent flashpoint. Large shares of LGBTQ+ students report being harassed or bullied, and those who face the most hostility are far more likely to skip school, struggle academically, and consider self-harm. The encouraging flip side is that protection works. In practical terms, a single accepting adult can meaningfully lower a young person's risk of danger and self-harm.

Progress and Backlash

The legal landscape has shifted dramatically within living memory. Homosexuality was classified as a mental illness in the United States until 1973, when it was removed from the official diagnostic manual (American Psychiatric Association, 2013)ReferenceAmerican Psychiatric Association. (2013). Diagnostic and statistical manual of mental disorders (5th ed.). American Psychiatric Publishing.. In 2003, the Supreme Court struck down remaining laws that criminalized same-sex intimacy (Lawrence v. Texas, 2003)ReferenceLawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003).. In 2015, the Court legalized same-sex marriage nationwide (Obergefell v. Hodges, 2015)ReferenceObergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015).. And in 2020, it ruled that firing someone for being gay or transgender is illegal sex discrimination under federal law (Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020)ReferenceBostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020).. Public opinion has moved alongside the law: a clear majority of Americans now support same-sex marriage and oppose discrimination based on orientation.

Progress has not been a straight line. Recent years have seen a wave of new legislation aimed at rolling back LGBTQ+ protections, frequently framed as protecting religious liberty. Much of this has focused on transgender people, including disputes over transgender youth's access to medical care, participation in sports, and use of gender-specific spaces. These are deeply contested areas of law and politics, with sincere arguments on multiple sides, and they remain unsettled.

Reducing Prejudice and Finding Support

Decades of research shows that prejudice can be reliably reduced by three factors. The first is simple education. Stereotypes can be busted simply by learning the truth about how LGBTQ+ people live, that queer people are extremely similar in their personalities, goals, and love lives to straight and cisgender people. At the end of the day, we're all people, deserving of compassion, love, and respect.

The second factor is personally getting to know LGBTQ+ people. This further breaks down stereotypes, but also engenders more positive attitudes toward this group over time. This is often called the contact hypothesisiContact hypothesisThe finding that continued, positive contact with members of a marginalized group tends to reduce prejudice against that group over time.View in glossary →, as continued and positive contact with a marginalized group is often enough on its own to significantly reduce prejudice. More and more people have an LGBTQ+ person in their lives — whether that's a family member, friend or neighbor. Nearly double that percentage know someone who is lesbian or gay, with 25% reporting they have an immediate family member who is LGBTQ+ (Mitchell, 2016; Orcés, 2023)ReferencesMitchell, T. (2016, September 28). Vast majority of Americans know someone who is gay, fewer know someone who is transgender. Pew Research Center.

Lastly, general visibility of LGBTQ+ in our culture at large has slowly but surely changed people's attitudes over time. Seeing gay and trans characters on TV has been shown to reduce stereotyping and prejudice, especially among people who don't have real-world contact with these groups (Schiappa et al., 2006)ReferenceSchiappa, E., Gregg, P. Key Terms

Androgens
A group of hormones, including testosterone, that guide male-typical development before birth and are central to the prenatal hormone theory of sexual orientation.
Androphilic
Sexually attracted to men. In the prenatal hormone theory, lower prenatal androgen exposure is linked to an androphilic pattern.
Bisexual
Describing a person attracted to more than one gender.
Cisgender
Describing a person whose gender identity matches the sex they were assigned at birth; such people are sometimes called a cis man or cis woman.
Coming out
The lifelong, repeated process of publicly acknowledging one's LGBTQ+ identity, which happens again and again because people are usually assumed to be straight.
Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)
A condition that exposes a developing fetus to unusually high levels of androgens; girls with CAH are somewhat more likely to be attracted to women, offering evidence for the prenatal hormone theory.
Contact hypothesis
The finding that continued, positive contact with members of a marginalized group tends to reduce prejudice against that group over time.
Conversion therapy
The discredited and harmful practice of trying to change a person's sexual orientation or gender identity; condemned by every major medical and mental-health association.
Epigenetics
Chemical tags that switch genes on or off without changing the DNA itself; epigenetic marks are one proposed influence on the development of sexual orientation.
Fraternal birth order effect
The reliable finding that gay men have, on average, more older biological brothers than straight men; each older brother raises the odds of being gay by roughly a third.
Gay
Attracted to the same sex or gender; often used for men specifically, and also as a broad term for anyone who is not heterosexual.
Gay-Straight Alliance
A student-led school club (also called a Gender and Sexuality Alliance) that supports LGBTQ+ students and is linked to better mental-health outcomes.
Gender dysphoria
The clinically significant distress that can arise from a mismatch between a person's body and their gender identity.
Gender fluidity
Flexibility or change over time in a person's own internal sense of their gender; documented most clearly in longitudinal studies of children and adolescents.
Gender identity
A person's own internal sense of being a man, a woman, both, or neither; distinct from sexual orientation.
Gender-affirming hormone therapy
Medical treatment that uses hormones to shift a person's secondary sex characteristics closer to their gender identity (estrogen and testosterone suppression for a trans woman; testosterone for a trans man).
Gender-affirming surgery
Surgical procedures, pursued by a minority of transgender people, that bring the body into closer alignment with a person's gender identity.
Gynephilic
Sexually attracted to women. In the prenatal hormone theory, higher prenatal androgen exposure is linked to a gynephilic pattern.
Heteronormativity
The built-in assumption that everyone is, or should be, heterosexual; another term for heterosexism.
Heterosexism
The built-in assumption that everyone is, or should be, heterosexual; usually unintentional but marginalizing. Also called heteronormativity.
Heterosexual
Attracted to a different sex or gender; commonly called "straight."
Homophobia
A pattern of fear, discomfort, or hostility toward gay people.
Homosexual
Attracted to the same sex or gender; commonly called "gay." The clinical-sounding term itself is fairly modern, coined in the late 1800s.
Hypothalamus
A small brain region involved in regulating sexual behavior; certain differences in it have been linked, on average, to sexual orientation.
Internalized homophobia
The turning inward of society's anti-gay messages by LGBTQ+ people themselves, which can produce shame, anxiety, and depression.
Kinsey Scale
A 0-to-6 scale, introduced by Alfred Kinsey, placing people along a continuum from exclusively heterosexual to exclusively homosexual.
Klein Sexual Orientation Grid
Fritz Klein's tool that measures orientation across several ingredients — attraction, behavior, fantasy, emotional and social preference, self-label, and change over time.
Lesbian
A woman attracted to other women.
Minority stress model
The model holding that the higher rates of mental-health problems among LGBTQ+ people are caused by chronic stigma, rejection, and discrimination rather than by their identity.
Pansexual
Attracted to people of all genders, with gender mattering little to the attraction.
Parasocial contact hypothesis
The idea that even one-sided "contact" with likable media figures, such as characters on television, can reduce prejudice much like real-world contact.
Prenatal hormone theory
The best-supported biological account of sexual orientation, proposing that the level of androgens a fetus is exposed to before birth shapes later attraction.
Queer
An umbrella term embraced by many who are not straight or gender-conforming; once a slur, now widely reclaimed, though not everyone is comfortable with it.
Sexual fluidity
Real change over time in a person's attractions, behavior, or identity; somewhat more common on average in women.
Sexual orientation
An enduring pattern of emotional, romantic, or sexual attraction to others.
Sexual prejudice
A broad term used by scientists for any form of negative attitude or prejudice directed at people because of their sexual orientation.
Social transition
Changing the outward, everyday aspects of gender presentation — such as name, pronouns, clothing, and documents — without any medical intervention; largely reversible.
Transfeminine
An umbrella term for people assigned male at birth who move in a feminine direction, especially those who do not identify strictly as women.
Transgender
Describing a person whose gender identity differs from the sex they were assigned at birth; a transgender person can have any sexual orientation.
Transitioning
The process of bringing one's outward life and body into alignment with one's true gender identity, which may unfold across social, legal, and medical dimensions.
Transmasculine
An umbrella term for people assigned female at birth who move in a masculine direction, especially those who do not identify strictly as men.
Trans man
A person assigned female at birth who identifies and lives as a man; also referred to as female-to-male, or FTM.
Trans woman
A person assigned male at birth who identifies and lives as a woman; also referred to as male-to-female, or MTF.
Two-Spirit
A term used by some Indigenous North American peoples for individuals who blend masculine and feminine roles, historically often honored as spiritual leaders.
